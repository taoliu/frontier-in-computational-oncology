# Functional Genomics in Cancer

## Module overview

This module should teach students how bulk functional genomics assays become computational signal-detection problems in cancer. The core story is that ChIP-seq, ATAC-seq, CUT&RUN, CUT&Tag, and bulk chromatin interaction assays do not directly ŌĆ£find enhancersŌĆØ or ŌĆ£find TF binding.ŌĆØ They generate assay-specific count tracks or contact matrices whose structure is set by chemistry, fragment size, local background, sequence bias, mappability, antibody performance, and replicate quality. The computational task is therefore to turn biased readouts into calibrated peaks, domains, footprints, motifs, chromatin contacts, and finally regulatory annotations that are useful for cancer biology. In oncology, this matters because many clinically important mechanisms are regulatory rather than coding: lineage-state switching, enhancer activation, super-enhancer dependence, and enhancer hijacking can change subtype definitions, oncogene assignment, and therapeutic hypotheses even when DNA mutations alone are not enough. The module should stay focused on bulk assays and regulatory element discovery in tumors and model systems, treating single-cell and spatial assays only as short transition points to later weeks. ## Key computational problems and assumptions

**Assay design determines the statistical object.** ChIP-seq, CUT&RUN, and CUT&Tag produce enrichment tracks whose expected background depends on fragmentation, local chromatin context, and control choice. ATAC-seq produces paired-end fragments that also encode nucleosome structure, while Hi-C, capture Hi-C, PLAC-seq, and HiChIP produce sparse contact counts with strong genomic-distance decay. A good lecture should make students write down the observed random variable before they discuss algorithms. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn25search3Ņłéturn13search0Ņłéturn12search12Ņłéturn7search0Ņłéturn14search6Ņłü

**Background is not flat, and controls are imperfect.** MACS introduced dynamic local Poisson modeling because read density varies across the genome for technical reasons unrelated to the target. Later work showed that GC-content bias can create systematic errors and batch effects in ChIP-seq, and ENCODE blacklists remain an essential practical defense against anomalous high-signal regions. More recent work such as WACS makes the same lesson explicit: background estimation is itself a model-selection problem, not an afterthought. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn40search0Ņłéturn40search17Ņłéturn22search0Ņłéturn8search8Ņłéturn31search0Ņłü

**Peak shape is a modeling choice, not just a parameter choice.** Narrow TF peaks, broad histone domains, and multi-scale ATAC accessibility patterns are not well served by one universal caller. MACS and SPP are built around local enrichment and strand geometry, SICER-style methods around spatial clustering of diffuse marks, and HMMRATAC around a hidden-state representation of nucleosome-free and nucleosome-enriched fragments. CUT&RUN and CUT&Tag further complicate this because the background can be sparse enough that empirical-threshold approaches such as SEACR behave differently from generic ChIP-style callers. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn40search1Ņłéturn41search5Ņłéturn5search1Ņłéturn12search1Ņłéturn12search24Ņłü

**Reproducibility and calibration need to be taught explicitly.** IDR remains central because replicate agreement is not the same as overlap-by-eye, and ENCODE operationalized it for high-throughput peak sets. At the same time, later work such as RECAP showed that nominal peak-caller P values can be too optimistic because the same data are often used both to nominate candidate regions and to test significance. This is exactly the kind of verification issue graduate students should learn to spot. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn28search0Ņłéturn27search2Ņłéturn27search0Ņłéturn41search14Ņłü

**Motif analysis and footprinting rest on strong assumptions.** Motif enrichment depends on fair background sequence construction, motif database quality, and the fact that many TFs share similar motifs. Footprinting adds a harder assumption: bound TFs create a local depletion pattern distinguishable from enzyme sequence bias and local nucleosome effects. HINT-ATAC and TOBIAS exist because uncorrected Tn5 bias can look like a footprint, and newer bias-modeling work shows that this remains an active problem. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn39search0Ņłéturn6search12Ņłéturn6search0Ņłéturn21search0Ņłéturn21search22Ņłü

**Regulatory annotation is rule-based or state-based, but rarely causal by itself.** ENCODE cCREs are built by integrating accessibility with H3K4me3, H3K27ac, CTCF, and TF binding, while ChromHMM-like segmentation learns latent states from multi-mark combinations. Chromatin interaction callers such as FitHiC2, CHiCAGO, and MAPS then add long-range linkage, but even significant contacts do not prove functional enhancer-gene relationships. This module should therefore teach annotation as probabilistic evidence aggregation, not as final truth. ## Main algorithms and tools to teach

**Preprocessing and QC.** Students should learn the ENCODE logic for alignment, duplicate handling, library complexity, FRiP, TSS enrichment for ATAC-seq, blacklist filtering, and replicate handling. For ChIP-seq in particular, strand cross-correlation from the SPP framework remains conceptually important because it ties quality metrics to the bimodal shape of real binding events. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn8search0Ņłéturn10search2Ņłéturn11search2Ņłéturn11search10Ņłéturn27search13Ņłéturn40search1Ņłü

**Peak calling and domain calling.** The core teaching set should be MACS3 for narrow peaks and general enrichment, SPP for score-track thinking and quality diagnostics, SICER or SICER2 for broad domains, and HMMRATAC for bulk ATAC-seq. For low-background tethered assays, SEACR and GoPeaks are the clearest examples of how CUT&RUN and CUT&Tag alter algorithm design. WACS is worth showing as a modern extension of MACS-style logic when control selection itself becomes a modeling problem. ## Representative datasets, benchmarks, and cancer case studies

**Representative datasets and benchmarks.** The best teaching datasets are the ones with orthogonal support and mature processing standards: ENCODE TF ChIP-seq, histone ChIP-seq, and bulk ATAC-seq processed through the uniform pipelines; Cistrome DB v3 for broad public reuse; TCGA bulk ATAC-seq for primary human tumor accessibility; and the recent TCGA-related 3D genome resource for primary tumors. Together they let students see both controlled benchmark-style analyses and difficult primary-cancer applications. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn8search0Ņłéturn8search11Ņłéturn8search6Ņłéturn35search1Ņłü

**Benchmark papers worth assigning or mining.** The 2024 benchmark of ATAC-seq and CUT&Tag processing strategies is useful for showing that software choice, peak shape, and control availability change performance. Recent CUT&RUN and CUT&Tag benchmark papers are also good because they show that assay-specific parameter choices matter and that concordance to ENCODE ChIP-seq is substantial but incomplete, especially outside the strongest peaks. Classical ChIP-seq benchmarks remain useful for teaching what features distinguish good callers on narrow versus broad data. **Pan-cancer accessibility as a discovery engine.** Corces and colleagues profiled 410 TCGA tumors across 23 cancer types and identified 562,709 accessible elements, extending the known cis-regulatory catalog and using chromatin accessibility to illuminate subtype structure, germline risk loci, and regulatory programs in primary cancers. Follow-up TCGA analysis showed that accessibility also carries clinical information, and later work connected primary-cancer accessibility maps to regional mutational processes more strongly than normal-tissue maps in many tumor types. **Cistromic reprogramming in prostate cancer.** Prostate cancer is a strong lecture example because it shows how bulk ChIP-seq changed biological interpretation from ŌĆ£same receptor, same programŌĆØ to extensive androgen-receptor cistrome rewiring during tumorigenesis and progression. Reviews from the last few years frame this as a general lesson in lineage TF cooperation, chromatin remodelers, and treatment-driven state change rather than a simple gain-or-loss of one factor. **Super-enhancer-defined states in neuroblastoma.** H3K27ac-based enhancer profiling showed that neuroblastoma contains at least two major super-enhancer-associated regulatory identities, often summarized as adrenergic and mesenchymal or related subclasses. This shifted interpretation from a largely mutation-centric disease view toward regulatory-state plasticity and lineage circuitry, and it remains an excellent bulk regulatory genomics case because the signal is visible in conventional ChIP-seq and enhancer annotation rather than only in single-cell data. **Enhancer hijacking in pediatric and acute leukemias.** Medulloblastoma became a landmark case when chromosomal rearrangements were shown to activate GFI1 and GFI1B through enhancer hijacking. More recently, enhancer hijacking has defined specific acute leukemia entities, including BCL11B-driven leukemias and newer AML examples in which active enhancer repertoires and structural variation together reveal oncogene activation that standard gene-centric interpretation would miss. These are powerful examples for linking peaks, chromatin state, and 3D contacts. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn16search3Ņłéturn18search6Ņłéturn18search20Ņłéturn18search0Ņłéturn18search16Ņłü

In parallel, HAPI showed how HiChIP-based analysis can detect enhancer hijacking caused by complex structural alterations and ecDNA, and even recent FFPE-compatible Hi-C studies suggest that bulk chromatin interaction mapping is getting closer to clinical biopsy workflows. ## Suggested three-lecture outline

1. **Background and problem definition.** Start from assay chemistry and data structure: cross-linking and immunoprecipitation in ChIP-seq, transposition in ATAC-seq, tethered nuclease or tagmentation in CUT&RUN and CUT&Tag, and ligation-based contact capture in chromatin interaction assays. Then define the biological need in cancer: to find active promoters, enhancers, insulators, TF occupancy, and long-range regulatory contacts that explain lineage state, subtype, risk loci, and oncogene activation when coding variation alone is insufficient. End the lecture by stating the three core computational questions: what is signal, what is background, and how do we know a call is reproducible. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn26search0Ņłéturn25search3Ņłéturn13search0Ņłéturn12search12Ņłéturn15search1Ņłü

2. **Method development and verification.** Move from read alignments to peak or loop calls. Teach MACS and SPP as the narrow-peak baseline, SICER-style clustering for broad domains, HMMRATAC for ATAC-specific structure, and SEACR or GoPeaks for sparse CUT data. Then cover motif enrichment, footprinting, and selected chromatin interaction statistics such as FitHiC2 and MAPS. Verification should include ENCODE-style QC and IDR, simulated nulls and calibration issues, benchmark papers, motif precision, overlap with orthogonal marks, and cancer-specific validation with expression or CRISPR follow-up when available. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn40search0Ņłéturn40search1Ņłéturn41search5Ņłéturn5search1Ņłéturn12search1Ņłéturn7search0Ņłéturn14search6Ņłéturn28search0Ņłéturn41search14Ņłéturn23search8Ņłü

3. **Applications and state of the field.** Use ENCODE, SCREEN, and Cistrome to show how public resources support cancer regulatory annotation at scale. Then build through case studies: TCGA pan-cancer ATAC, prostate AR cistrome rewiring, neuroblastoma super-enhancer states, enhancer hijacking in medulloblastoma and leukemia, and primary-cancer 3D genome maps. Close by discussing practical limits in primary tumors, current assay-specific biases, and why single-cell, spatial, and causal network modules are needed next but should not replace the bulk-method foundations taught here. ## Open problems, future directions, and boundaries

**Open problems and future directions.** The most important unsolved issues are still methodological. Peak callers and loop callers need better uncertainty calibration, especially when candidate-region selection and testing are not cleanly separated. Bias models also remain assay-specific and incomplete: ChIP-seq still suffers from GC and regional background effects, ATAC-seq footprinting still depends strongly on Tn5 bias correction, and CUT&Tag appears to have its own accessibility-coupled biases that are only beginning to get dedicated correction methods. Benchmarking is improving, but ground truth is still usually indirect, based on motif overlap, concordance with ENCODE peaks, gene-expression correlations, or limited perturbation assays rather than true site-level labels. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn22search0Ņłéturn21search0Ņłéturn24search12Ņłéturn41search14Ņłéturn24search14Ņłéturn24search20Ņłü

A realistic near-future direction for this module is to show students how bulk regulatory assays are becoming more clinically usable without pretending that they are already routine diagnostics. Low-input and small-sample chromatin profiling is stronger than it was a few years ago, FFPE-compatible 3D methods are emerging, super-enhancer and cCRE resources are getting larger, and modern peak callers increasingly include machine-learning components. The teaching goal should therefore be to give students durable method principles that transfer across new protocols rather than to anchor the week around one fast-moving software stack. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn24search14Ņłéturn36search16Ņłéturn15search7Ņłéturn30search22Ņłü

**Boundaries for this week.** This module should include bulk assay physics, QC, enrichment modeling, peak and domain calling, motif analysis, footprinting, ENCODE or Cistrome-style regulatory annotation, and selected bulk chromatin interaction methods that support enhancer discovery in cancer. It should also include cancer case studies where regulatory element discovery changed interpretation of subtype, lineage state, or oncogene assignment.

It should leave most single-cell chromatin accessibility and single-cell CUT methods to Week 3, spatial epigenomics and spatial regulatory localization to Week 4, and network-level causal inference, enhancer-gene causal modeling, and larger gene-regulatory network reconstruction to Week 6. Brief transition slides are useful, but the main pedagogic value of this week is to establish bulk assay-aware statistical thinking before students move to higher-dimensional settings.

# Frontier in Computational Oncology Week 9 Computational Pathology and Cancer Imaging

## Module overview

This module tells a coherent story from digitized tissue images to morphology-derived cancer phenotypes. Its core data modality is the whole-slide image: a multi-resolution, gigapixel tissue representation whose computational structure is very different from a gene-expression matrix. A representative slide scanned at 0.25 μm/pixel can be about 80,000 × 60,000 pixels and ~15 GB uncompressed, which forces algorithm designers to make explicit choices about microns-per-pixel, tiling, context windows, aggregation, storage formats, and visualization. In oncology, this matters because morphology remains central to diagnosis, grading, staging support, prognostication, biomarker quantification, and increasingly to predicting molecular alterations and treatment response directly from H&E, IHC, and multiplex tissue images. The key unmet computational challenges are annotation scarcity, noisy slide-level labels, stain and scanner variation, multiple-instance learning over variable-length bags of tiles, multi-scale representation learning, and the mismatch between excellent retrospective performance and the much harder problem of external, clinical, and regulatory validation. This week should therefore teach not “how to run a pathology model,” but how to formulate, build, benchmark, and validate image-native methods that can turn raw morphology into robust cancer phenotypes.

## Must-read papers and resources

- **Song, Jaume, Williamson, Lu, Vaidya, Miller, Mahmood, 2023, *Nature Reviews Bioengineering*** — likely the best single technical review to assign at the start of the week. It organizes computational pathology by task class, data regime, and deployment challenge, and is especially good for motivating why pathology images are not just another computer-vision benchmark. DOI: **10.1038/s44222-023-00096-8**.

- **Aggarwal, Bharadwaj, Corredor, Pathak, Badve, Madabhushi, 2025, *Nature Reviews Clinical Oncology*** — a valuable “reality check” on what has and has not translated clinically, with attention to validation, regulation, implementation, and economics. It is an excellent capstone reading for the third lecture. DOI: **10.1038/s41571-025-00991-6**.

- **Campanella et al., 2019, *Nature Medicine*** — landmark weak-supervision paper showing that slide-level labels from routine diagnoses can train clinically strong models without dense manual annotation. It is still the canonical entry point for why multiple-instance learning matters in pathology. DOI: **10.1038/s41591-019-0508-1**.

- **Lu et al., 2021, *Nature Biomedical Engineering*** — the CLAM paper. Essential for teaching attention-based MIL, instance-level clustering, data efficiency, and interpretable region discovery under slide-level supervision. DOI: **10.1038/s41551-020-00682-w**.

- **Graham et al., 2019, *Medical Image Analysis*** — HoVer-Net remains a foundational segmentation paper for nuclei instance segmentation plus cell-type classification, and a good bridge between classical segmentation losses and pathology-specific instance reasoning. DOI: **10.1016/j.media.2019.101563**.

- **Bulten et al., 2022, *Nature Medicine*** — the PANDA challenge paper. Essential for benchmarking, reproducibility, and the problem of grading prostate biopsies at international scale. DOI: **10.1038/s41591-021-01620-2**.

- **Coudray et al., 2018, *Nature Medicine*** — the landmark lung cancer paper showing subtype classification and mutation prediction from H&E. Still one of the clearest demonstrations of image–molecular association from routine histology. DOI: **10.1038/s41591-018-0177-5**.

- **Kather et al., 2019, *Nature Medicine*** — the canonical MSI-from-histology paper in gastrointestinal cancer, and one of the cleanest examples of turning routine slides into a screening biomarker for a therapeutically actionable molecular phenotype. DOI: **10.1038/s41591-019-0462-y**.

- **Diao et al., 2021, *Nature Communications*** — important because it tackles interpretability directly through human-interpretable image features rather than only heatmaps, linking morphology and tumor microenvironment structure to molecular phenotypes. DOI: **10.1038/s41467-021-21896-9**.

- **Chen et al., 2024, *Nature Medicine*** — UNI is the clearest entry point into pathology self-supervised learning as an image-specific phenomenon: very large unlabeled WSI corpora, in-domain pretraining, and broad downstream transfer. DOI: **10.1038/s41591-024-02857-3**.

- **Xu et al., 2024, *Nature*** — Prov-GigaPath is important because it moves beyond tile encoders toward whole-slide foundation modeling and demonstrates very strong downstream performance across a broad pathology benchmark. DOI: **10.1038/s41586-024-07441-w**.

- **Neidlinger et al., 2025, *Nature Biomedical Engineering*** — one of the most important recent benchmark papers. It evaluates 19 pathology foundation models on 31 weakly supervised tasks using external cohorts not used for pretraining, making it ideal for teaching the difference between headline accuracy and trustworthy benchmarking. DOI: **10.1038/s41551-025-01516-3**.

## Computational problems and methods

**Key computational problems and assumptions.** The first problem is **scale**: whole-slide images are tiled, multi-resolution pyramids, not fixed-size tensors. Any WSI pipeline therefore assumes that local morphology can be sampled into patches and that slide-level information can later be reconstructed by aggregation. In practice, the most important imaging variable is often microns-per-pixel rather than the nominal “20× versus 40×” label, because morphology is scale dependent and scanner metadata are inconsistent.

A second problem is **appearance variability**. H&E color depends on staining chemistry, tissue preparation, fading, and scanner optics, so stain normalization or augmentation is often used to reduce nuisance variation. The hidden assumption is that normalization preserves biology while removing laboratory- or device-specific color shifts; this is helpful but never neutral, and recent reviews and domain-generalization surveys emphasize that no single strategy solves all shift types. The Macenko method is the classic landmark, while recent reviews compare larger families of stain-standardization methods and their failure modes.

A third problem is **label scarcity and weak supervision**. Dense pathology annotation is expensive and disagreement-prone, so many pipelines learn from case- or slide-level labels extracted from diagnostic reports. Multiple-instance learning formalizes this as learning over bags of tiles with only bag labels; attention-based and transformer-based variants differ mainly in how they relax the “independent patch” simplification and model inter-tile dependence and spatial context. CLAM emphasizes data-efficient attention with instance-level clustering; TransMIL explicitly addresses the inadequacy of an i.i.d. instance assumption; graph models such as Patch-GCN add local spatial structure for prognostic tasks.

A fourth problem is **segmentation and object definition**. In pathology, nuclei boundaries are ambiguous, touching cells are common, and “ground truth” is often a consensus rather than an absolute truth. Instance segmentation metrics such as Dice, AJI, panoptic quality, and F1 therefore matter because they determine whether downstream morphometrics or cell-composition features are trustworthy. This becomes even more critical in IHC and multiplex imaging, where cell segmentation errors directly corrupt biomarker quantification and cell-phenotyping analyses.

A fifth problem is **validation under distribution shift**. Scanner vendor, lab protocol, tissue fixation, case mix, and demographics all shift model behavior. Recent surveys and benchmarks make two points that are especially teachable: careful stain augmentation and experimental design can help, but there is no universal domain-generalization recipe; and pathology foundation models still require careful external evaluation rather than assuming robustness from scale alone.

**Main algorithms, models, and tools to teach.** For preprocessing and infrastructure, students should understand DICOM-WSI and vendor formats, whole-slide readers, patch extraction, magnification harmonization, stain normalization and augmentation, and batch visualization. In practice, the most useful open tools for demonstration are **TIAToolbox** for end-to-end pipelines, **Slideflow** for interactive deep-learning workflows on WSIs, **PathML** for standardized pathology preprocessing, and **QuPath** for annotation and quantification; these should be presented as research infrastructure rather than the substance of the week.

For segmentation, teach the progression from **U-Net** as the landmark biomedical segmentation architecture, to pathology-specific instance models such as **HoVer-Net**, and then to **Mesmer** for multiplex tissue segmentation. The conceptual emphasis should be on why pathology needs instance-level reasoning, not just semantic masks: touching nuclei, cell overlap, sparse positives, and the fact that downstream morphology features are often computed per object. For older landmarks, assigning short excerpts from **Macenko 2009** and **U-Net 2015** is enough; the main lecture time should go to pathology-specific descendants.

For slide-level learning, the key abstraction is **multiple-instance learning**. Students should compare simple bag pooling with **attention MIL**, **CLAM**, **TransMIL**, and **Patch-GCN**, and explicitly discuss what each assumes about dependence among tiles, spatial structure, and the relationship between tile evidence and bag labels. For prognosis, the important addition is a survival head such as a Cox-style or ranking-based objective atop an aggregation model, with attention to censoring assumptions and calibration rather than just the concordance index.

For representation learning, the methods to teach are **HIPT** for hierarchical self-supervised multi-scale modeling, **UNI** for large-scale pathology SSL, **CONCH** and **TITAN** for pathology image–text alignment, and **Virchow** or **Prov-GigaPath** as current whole-slide or large-scale foundation-model exemplars. The pedagogical boundary is important: in this week, these are image-specific examples of pretraining under gigapixel, stain-variable, weak-label conditions. The general theory of foundation models, cross-modal instruction tuning, and model governance should be deferred to Week 10.

For interpretation and biologic linkage, teach two contrasting approaches: **post hoc localization** from weakly supervised attention maps, and **intrinsically interpretable feature engineering** such as the human-interpretable image features of Diao et al. This contrast is useful because pathology needs more than saliency maps; it needs morphologic biomarkers that pathologists can audit, quantify, and compare to known cell and tissue patterns.

## Datasets, benchmarks, and validation

**Representative datasets and benchmarks.** The module should train students to recognize that no single dataset covers all pathology tasks. **TCGA** remains the canonical multi-cancer resource because it links pathology images to molecular and clinical data, and the **Cancer Digital Slide Archive** and **TCIA** make large portions of the pathology and paired imaging ecosystem practically usable. **CPTAC** collections on TCIA are particularly valuable when a project needs pathology paired with radiology, proteomics, and clinical endpoints.

For diagnostic slide-level benchmarking, **CAMELYON16/17** remains the landmark metastasis-detection challenge, both because of its hidden-test-set design and because the JAMA analysis showed how top deep-learning systems compared against pathologists. For prostate diagnosis and grading, **PANDA** is the obvious benchmark because it combines major international participation with clinically meaningful Gleason grading.

For nuclei and cell recognition, teach the complementary roles of **MoNuSeg** and **MoNuSAC** for generalized nucleus segmentation and classification, **CoNIC** for large-scale nuclear detection, segmentation, classification, and counting, and **NuCLS** for breast-cancer nucleus labels with substantial inter-rater structure. These datasets are especially good for teaching how annotation protocol, target ontology, and evaluation metric shape the resulting model.

For robustness under domain shift, **MIDOG 2022** is one of the best teaching datasets because it was built around cross-domain mitosis detection and includes an IHC-assisted reference standard, making it unusually useful for discussing ground-truth construction and external generalization. For multiplex cell segmentation, **TissueNet/Mesmer** is the right benchmark pair because it spans multiple organs and imaging platforms and directly exposes how segmentation quality propagates into downstream spatial phenotyping. **BigPicture** is worth introducing as a consortium-scale data infrastructure effort aimed at responsible, shared pathology AI development.

**Validation principles worth emphasizing.** Students should come away with a strong benchmark ethic: split by patient, then by site or scanner when possible; avoid hidden pretraining overlap; preserve external test sets; report calibration and operating points, not just AUROC; and treat molecular assays, IHC, or pathologist consensus as fallible reference standards rather than indisputable truth. This matters because a 2024 meta-analysis found high pooled sensitivity and specificity across pathology AI studies, yet also found substantial heterogeneity and that 99% of included studies had at least one area of high or unclear risk of bias or applicability concern. A 2025 review of external validation studies in lung pathology similarly concluded that the evidence base remains dominated by small or non-representative retrospective studies.

For reporting and clinical translation, this week should explicitly teach **TRIPOD+AI** for development/validation studies and **SPIRIT-AI / CONSORT-AI** for clinical trial protocols and reports. These frameworks make students specify the intended use, input/output handling, human–AI interaction, error analysis, and setting of deployment rather than merely optimizing metrics. The NCI’s 2024 workshop report is also useful here: it stresses infrastructure, standardization, de-identification, DICOM support, image-performance criteria, storage, and access governance as first-class research and trial design issues rather than afterthoughts.

## Cancer case studies and state of the field

**Cancer case studies.** In **breast cancer**, the CAMELYON line of work is still the best entry point for understanding how carefully designed hidden-test challenges can move a task from proof-of-concept to a diagnostic-accuracy conversation. For grading and prognosis, the more recent breast literature shows how morphology models are being extended from lesion detection toward grade components, recurrence risk, and tumor immune features, but these applications also make label quality and outcome confounding more obvious.

In **prostate cancer**, the PANDA challenge is the canonical example of computational pathology as a grading problem with international variability, imperfect labels, and clinically meaningful endpoints. It is a particularly good teaching case because it brings together weak labels, patch sampling, site bias, and human-level comparison in a task pathologists already understand deeply.

In **colorectal cancer**, the field offers perhaps the cleanest set of morphology-to-biomarker examples. **MSI prediction from H&E** is now a classic image-genomics case study, while **multistain deep learning for prognosis and therapy response** shows how routine morphology can be enriched by immune-marker staining and used to quantify tumor–immune architecture with clear clinical relevance. The latter is especially useful because it demonstrates that pathology AI is not limited to diagnosis; it can also generate an immune-morphology biomarker tied to response.

In **lung cancer**, the Coudray study remains a landmark for subtype and mutation prediction directly from H&E, and more recent treatment-response work extends the same idea toward inferring transcriptomics and therapy benefit from images. Methodologically, lung is also a cautionary case: despite strong retrospective performance for subtype and related tasks, a 2025 external-validation review concluded that robust real-world validation remains sparse.

For **tumor microenvironment morphology and pan-cancer image–molecular association**, Diao’s interpretable feature framework and the CoNIC challenge are valuable complements. Diao et al. show that interpretable tissue and cell features can predict clinically relevant molecular phenotypes and checkpoint-related signatures, while CoNIC demonstrates how improving cell recognition quality materially improves downstream morphometric biomarkers and highlights immune-cell classes such as eosinophils and neutrophils in colon tissue analysis. In IHC, automated **H-score** quantification is now strong enough to be worth teaching as a serious image-analysis problem rather than a niche tool application.

**Application and state of the field.** The current frontier is dominated by **pathology foundation models** and **self-supervised pretraining** over very large corpora of unlabeled or weakly labeled slides, sometimes aligned with reports or captions. UNI, CONCH, Virchow, Prov-GigaPath, CHIEF, and TITAN differ in whether they learn at tile level, slide level, or image–text level, but they all try to solve the same pathology-native problems: scarce annotation, multi-scale context, and generalization across tissues and tasks. Crucially, recent multi-center benchmarks show that publicly available pathology foundation models can substantially improve downstream performance, but they also show that model rankings vary by task, that leakage-aware evaluation matters, and that “larger and pretrained” is not a substitute for sound benchmarking.

Clinical translation, however, is behind method development. A 2024 review of commercially available pathology AI found a growing set of approved H&E products in Europe/Great Britain but emphasized how hard it remains to find public evidence on clinical validation, while the FDA’s AI-enabled device list and public summaries improve transparency only partially. The upshot for a graduate course is that “state of the field” must be taught as a two-speed system: extremely rapid representation-learning progress, but slow and uneven progress in prospective validation, workflow integration, and public evidence quality.

## Lecture plan, future directions, and module boundaries

**Suggested three-lecture outline.**
**Lecture one: Background and problem definition.** Start with the clinical role of pathology in oncology, then explain what a WSI physically is: pyramid levels, microns-per-pixel, color space, scanner metadata, file formats, tiling, tissue detection, and why image pipelines differ from molecular-matrix pipelines. Then organize the task taxonomy: diagnosis, grading, segmentation, cell composition, prognosis, biomarker quantification, and image–molecular association across H&E, IHC, and multiplex tissue images.

**Lecture two: Method development and verification.** Teach segmentation and instance modeling first, using U-Net, HoVer-Net, and Mesmer to motivate the shift from pixels to cells. Then teach weak supervision over tiles with MIL, CLAM, TransMIL, and Patch-GCN; finish with pathology-specific SSL and foundation models as feature extractors. The validation block should be explicit: simulation versus hidden-test-set benchmarking, assay-backed labels, patient/site/scanner splits, survival metrics, calibration, and reporting standards such as TRIPOD+AI and CONSORT-AI.

**Lecture three: Applications and state of the field.** Use prostate grading, breast metastasis detection, colorectal MSI and immune-morphology prediction, lung histology-genomics, and IHC/multiplex quantification as the application spine. End with foundation-model benchmarks, product evidence, NCI workshop recommendations, and a discussion of what counts as convincing oncology evidence for an imaging AI system.

**Open problems and future directions.** The most important methodological problem is still **robust external generalization** across scanners, staining pipelines, institutions, and patient populations; the domain-generalization literature is explicit that there is no one-size-fits-all solution. The second is **trustworthy benchmarking**: hidden overlap between pretraining and evaluation data, selective reporting, and over-reliance on retrospective AUC remain major concerns. The third is **multi-scale modeling efficiency**: pathology needs models that preserve fine nuclear detail and broad tissue architecture without exploding memory or compute. The fourth is **interpretability**: the field still needs better morphologic concepts, not just prettier heatmaps. The fifth is **clinical evidence generation**: prospective trials, workflow studies, human–AI interaction analyses, post-deployment monitoring, and transparent product registries are still comparatively rare. The sixth is **interoperability and governance**, including DICOM support, de-identification, access control, and sustainable storage/compute for very large image archives.

**Notes on boundaries.** What belongs in this week is the computation of **visual and morphologic tissue phenotypes** from H&E, IHC, and multiplex images treated primarily as images: tiling, stain handling, segmentation, weak supervision, morphology representation learning, slide-level diagnosis/prognosis, image biomarkers, and image–molecular association. A brief radiology tie-in is appropriate only when it clarifies cross-scale morphology, for example through paired radiology–pathology resources or radiopathomics examples, but the week should not become a general radiomics module.

What should be left to other weeks is equally important. **Spatial omics molecular neighborhood analysis** belongs to Week 4, even when a tissue image is present, because the primary signal there is molecularly resolved cell or neighborhood structure rather than a morphology-only phenotype. **Clinical EHR or outcomes modeling** belongs to Week 8, except for the narrow question of how pathology models are validated against clinical endpoints. **General foundation-model theory** belongs to Week 10; in Week 9, foundation models should appear only as pathology-specific representation-learning examples motivated by gigapixel slides, weak labels, and pathology-report alignment.

# Week 11 Module Research for Generative AI, Agents, and Knowledge Systems for Oncology

## Module overview

This module should tell a very specific story: oncology is no longer a setting where a pretrained model is simply queried for static medical knowledge, but one in which modern systems retrieve current evidence, normalize biomarker and disease entities, call APIs and databases, orchestrate multi-step workflows, attach provenance, and keep humans in the loop for adjudication. The biological and clinical need is obvious: oncology knowledge changes rapidly with new biomarker evidence, trial criteria, and drug approvals, while the key source materials are fragmented across papers, guidelines, trial registries, pathology reports, clinic notes, and structured knowledge bases. Recent oncology reviews show that much early LLM work focused on off-the-shelf question answering, whereas the newer frontier is interactive systems such as RAG over oncology knowledge resources, tool-using agents, and workflow automation for trial matching, tumor board support, and data curation. A strong graduate module should therefore emphasize system design and verification—retrieval pipelines, tool use, knowledge graphs, provenance and citation evaluation, expert-grounded validation, and privacy-aware implementation—rather than generic foundation model pretraining, which belongs to Week 10.

## Must-read papers and resources

**Carl et al., *npj Precision Oncology* (2024), “Large language model use in clinical oncology,” plus Hao et al., *npj Digital Medicine* (2025), “Large language model integrations in cancer decision-making: a systematic review and meta-analysis.”** Read these together as the field-mapping entry point. Carl et al. review 34 peer-reviewed oncology studies through March 2024 and show that most early papers evaluated static oncologic question answering; Hao et al. extend the picture to 56 studies across 15 cancer types and are especially useful for discussing how systems are actually integrated and evaluated in cancer decision workflows. DOI: 10.1038/s41698-024-00733-4; DOI: 10.1038/s41746-025-01824-7.

**Yang et al., *npj Health Systems* (2025), “Retrieval-augmented generation for generative artificial intelligence in health care.”** This is the clean architectural foundation for Lecture 1 or the start of Lecture 2. It formalizes the indexing–retrieval–generation pipeline and frames why RAG matters in medicine: freshness, reliability, personalization, and reduced dependence on stale parametric memory. DOI: 10.1038/s44401-024-00004-1.

**Xiong et al., Findings of ACL (2024), “Benchmarking Retrieval-Augmented Generation for Medicine.”** This is one of the most important methods papers for the module because it introduces the medical MIRAGE benchmark and the MedRAG toolkit, showing how corpus choice, retriever choice, and model choice interact. It is especially valuable pedagogically because it reports concrete implementation lessons, including gains over chain-of-thought prompting and the “lost-in-the-middle” effect in medical RAG. ArXiv:2402.13178.

**Wu et al., *Nature Communications* (2025), “An automated framework for assessing how well LLMs cite relevant medical references.”** This is the core provenance paper to assign. SourceCheckup moves the evaluation target from answer fluency to statement-level evidentiary support, and it shows that citation presence is not enough: many medical LLM outputs still cite papers that fail to support, or even contradict, their claims. DOI: 10.1038/s41467-025-58551-6.

**Jin et al., *Bioinformatics* (2024), “GeneGPT.”** If the module needs one canonical paper on biomedical tool use, this is it. GeneGPT prompts a model to use NCBI web APIs and outperforms previous baselines on GeneTuring; it is ideal for teaching when language models should become API-calling systems rather than pure text generators.

**Matsumoto et al., *Bioinformatics* (2025), “ESCARGOT,” together with Mehandru et al., *Scientific Reports* (2025), “BioAgents.”** Assign these together for the agentic systems lecture. ESCARGOT shows knowledge-graph-guided, graph-of-thought reasoning for biomedical questions; BioAgents shows multi-agent workflow support for bioinformatics using smaller, fine-tuned models plus RAG. Together they illustrate two distinct but complementary design patterns: reasoning over structured knowledge versus orchestrating end-to-end analysis workflows. DOI for ESCARGOT is available through the journal article; BioAgents is in *Scientific Reports* and is useful as a practical workflow automation counterpoint.

**Jin et al., *Nature Communications* (2024), “Matching patients to clinical trials with large language models.”** TrialGPT is the clearest oncology-specific agentic workflow paper in the literature. It decomposes patient-to-trial matching into retrieval, criterion-level matching, and ranking, and it reports large-scale synthetic annotations plus user-study evidence for time savings. DOI: 10.1038/s41467-024-53081-z.

**Sushil et al., *NEJM AI* (2024), “CORAL: Expert-Curated Oncology Reports to Advance Language Model Inference.”** This is a strong paper for teaching oncology text structuring and annotation. Its value is not just the dataset but the schema: oncology notes require more than named entities—they require temporality, treatment history, modifiers, and relations. DOI: 10.1056/AIdbp2300110.

**Lammert et al., *JCO Precision Oncology* (2024), “Expert-Guided Large Language Models for Clinical Decision Support in Precision Oncology.”** MEREDITH is a good case study in domain-tailored evidence integration. It starts from a general-purpose backbone, then improves utility by adding tumor-board-like evidence retrieval over literature, trials, approval status, and guidelines. It is especially useful for classroom discussion of what “expert-guided” really means operationally. DOI: 10.1200/PO-24-00478.

**Jun et al., *Cancer Cell* (2026), “A context-augmented large language model for accurate precision oncology medicine recommendations.”** This is currently one of the strongest oncology RAG case studies. It uses Molecular Oncology Almanac as the retrieval backbone and reports very high accuracy on synthetic and real-world oncologist queries, making it a natural anchor for the application lecture. DOI: 10.1016/j.ccell.2025.12.017.

**Reardon et al., *Nature Cancer* (2021), “Integrating molecular profiles into clinical frameworks through the Molecular Oncology Almanac,” together with the MOAlmanac resource.** Although slightly older than the preferred window, this is a landmark knowledge-system paper for precision oncology. It gives students a concrete example of a curated, updatable clinicogenomic knowledge base that later RAG systems can actually retrieve from. DOI: 10.1038/s43018-021-00243-3.

**Resource bundle for knowledge-grounded oncology systems: OncoKB, CIViC, PrimeKG, and SPOKE.** These are not interchangeable. OncoKB focuses on clinically actionable cancer variants and evidence levels; CIViC is an open, community-curated cancer variant interpretation resource; PrimeKG is a multimodal precision medicine knowledge graph spanning diseases, drugs, and biological scales; and SPOKE is a large, provenance-rich biomedical knowledge graph with an API and weekly rebuilds. Together they define the “knowledge systems” half of this module.

## Key computational problems and assumptions

**Freshness and temporal grounding.** Oncology recommendations age quickly because approvals, trial eligibility criteria, and variant evidence change continuously. The core assumption of this module is therefore that many oncology questions are not answerable safely from model parameters alone; the system has to retrieve temporally appropriate evidence at inference time and ideally expose the evidence version or source date to the user. This is exactly why RAG-style systems are attractive in oncology and why context-augmented precision oncology systems now use curated resources such as MOAlmanac instead of relying only on pretrained knowledge.

**Entity normalization across heterogeneous evidence.** A working oncology system must map messy natural language into canonical disease, gene, variant, drug, and phenotype identifiers. The hidden assumption in many papers is that this normalization step is either solved or cheaply approximated, but in practice it determines recall and faithfulness. Knowledge-graph and knowledge-base papers make the issue visible: PrimeKG, SPOKE, OncoKB, CIViC, and MOAlmanac all exist partly because biomedical knowledge is fragmented across ontologies, repositories, and text.

**Retrieval granularity and evidence fusion.** The system designer must decide whether to retrieve full papers, abstract snippets, guideline paragraphs, trial eligibility clauses, graph neighborhoods, or API-returned structured facts. That choice encodes assumptions about what counts as sufficient evidence. Work such as MedRAG, i-MedRAG, RAG², KG-RAG, and MedGraphRAG shows that performance depends heavily on chunk selection, distractor filtering, hybrid retrieval, and multi-step follow-up querying rather than on the generator alone.

**Provenance is stronger than citation.** A citation string next to an answer does not mean the answer is supported. The critical assumption for this module is that provenance must be checked at the statement level: the cited source has to entail or at least support the specific claim. SourceCheckup and the TREC BioGen efforts are important because they operationalize this distinction and make grounded generation a measurable system goal.

**Tool use changes the unit of evaluation.** The relevant question is no longer only “Was the answer correct?” but also “Did the system query the right tool, with the right arguments, in the right order, and interpret the output correctly?” GeneGPT, BioRAGent, and TrialGPT exemplify this shift: the model becomes a controller over APIs, databases, and retrieval modules. The implicit assumption is that external tools are higher-trust sources than the backbone model; the challenge is that the orchestration logic can still fail.

**Human evaluation is unavoidable because oncology often permits multiple valid answers.** Tumor-board recommendations, trial matches, and literature-grounded therapy suggestions frequently admit more than one defensible output. That means exact match or benchmark accuracy cannot be the only objective. Oncology reviews repeatedly note evaluation heterogeneity, while papers such as MEREDITH and TrialGPT use expert adjudication or similarity-to-expert judgments precisely because the true target is usefulness under uncertainty, not a single canonical string.

**Privacy and architecture are linked.** When systems touch pathology reports, progress notes, or other PHI-bearing corpora, architecture choices are constrained by privacy. That pushes some workflows toward de-identification, on-premise deployment, local models, or tightly governed retrieval layers. Reviews of patient privacy in healthcare LLMs, and oncology/pathology extraction studies that explicitly favor local deployment, make this a design constraint rather than an afterthought.

## Main algorithms, models, and tools to teach

**Classic RAG and the medical-specific variants.** Students should first learn the canonical index–retrieve–generate stack, then move quickly to medical variants that expose the real failure modes. MedRAG/MIRAGE is the cleanest benchmark-driven teaching example; i-MedRAG introduces iterative follow-up questions for complex medical reasoning; RAG² shows how rationale-guided retrieval and filtering can reduce distractor sensitivity and corpus bias. These papers make clear that method development lives in retriever design, query rewriting, snippet selection, and evidence aggregation as much as in prompting.

**Graph-backed and knowledge-base-backed retrieval.** This module should explicitly teach when keyword or embedding retrieval is not enough. KG-RAG, MedGraphRAG, and KRAGEN show how graphs can encode multihop biomedical relations, provide more structured provenance, and let systems retrieve neighborhood-level context instead of loose text chunks. In oncology, this matters because biomarker–drug–tumor-type relationships are naturally graph-structured and often need controlled vocabularies and evidence levels rather than free-text similarity alone.

**Tool-augmented reasoning and API calling.** GeneGPT is the canonical teaching case for biomedical tool use: the model retrieves through NCBI APIs rather than hallucinating answers. Students should learn the design space here—tool schemas, argument generation, error handling, and answer composition from structured results—and then compare it with broader multi-agent biomedical assistants such as BioRAGent, where guide, retriever, and reviewer roles are separated explicitly.

**Agentic orchestration.** The right abstraction for week 11 is not “agents are smarter chatbots,” but “agents are control systems for multi-step scientific work.” ESCARGOT, BioAgents, and broader biomedical agent reviews provide good exemplars: planning, memory, external tools, verification loops, and role-specialized agents. A useful classroom contrast is the oncology evaluation in multiple myeloma showing that an agentic workflow can outperform a simpler RAG system on relevance and comprehensiveness while still remaining far from high clinical-use readiness.

**Knowledge resources and ontologies as first-class system components.** Students should treat MOAlmanac, OncoKB, CIViC, PrimeKG, and SPOKE not as optional add-ons but as computational assets. What to teach is how each resource differs: assertion evidence levels, open versus restricted access, graph versus tabular structure, API availability, update cadence, and suitability for retrieval, ranking, or graph traversal. In practice, the most robust oncology systems will mix several of these resources rather than depend on one.

**Validation and guardrail tooling.** The module should teach evaluation frameworks alongside model architectures. SourceCheckup is essential for citation support; TREC BioGen is the main community benchmark for biomedical grounded generation; CREOLA shows how to move from vague “hallucination” discussions to explicit taxonomies of errors, omissions, and potential harm. If time permits, older landmarks such as Lewis et al. on RAG and ReAct on interleaved reasoning-and-acting make a good historical bridge from Week 10 fundamentals to Week 11 systems.

## Representative datasets and benchmarks

**Biomedical QA and explanation benchmarks.** MultiMedQA, MedQA, PubMedQA, BioASQ, and MedExQA are still useful, but their pedagogical role in this module should be specific: they are stress tests for reasoning, retrieval, explanation quality, and evaluation design, not substitutes for oncology workflow validation. MedExQA is especially helpful because it centers multiple explanations rather than single short answers, which aligns better with evidence-centered biomedical reasoning.

**RAG and provenance benchmarks.** MIRAGE/MedRAG should be the default benchmark for comparing retrieval corpora, retrievers, and LLM backbones in medical RAG. TREC BioGen 2024 and 2025 are valuable because they explicitly evaluate biomedical generative retrieval and, in 2025, grounding with citations that support answer sentences and whole answers. SourceCheckup and BioACE can then be discussed as automated evaluation layers that move closer to claim-level source validation.

**Oncology-specific corpora and task datasets.** CORAL gives students a small but richly annotated oncology note corpus for extracting patient, tumor, treatment, and temporal information. The UCSF breast pathology report dataset contributes 769 manually labeled reports across 12 clinically relevant tasks and demonstrates that GPT-4 zero-shot inference can rival or exceed supervised baselines on some categories. TrialGPT contributes an evaluation corpus of 183 synthetic patients with over 75,000 trial annotations for patient-to-trial matching. For biomarker curation from real-world oncology records, the PD-L1 extraction study is a practical example of how unstructured EHR data become a research-ready oncology variable.

**Knowledge-system and workflow benchmarks.** GeneTuring is the strongest benchmark here for tool-using genomics question answering; ClinicalTrials.gov is the obvious live corpus for trial discovery and matching; and metadata harmonization studies show how LLMs can standardize inconsistent researcher-written dataset metadata, including in cancer-adjacent domains. Emerging agent benchmarks such as BioAgent Bench are worth mentioning as near-future infrastructure, especially if the course wants students to think beyond QA and toward reproducible workflow execution.

## Cancer case studies and suggested lecture arc

**Clinical trial matching and trial-criteria structuring.** TrialGPT is the strongest flagship case study because it decomposes an oncology workflow into retrieval, eligibility reasoning, and ranking, then validates each stage. It is also worth pairing with newer work on adapting TrialGPT to real-world eligibility screening and with studies that extract biomarkers from oncology trials, because together they show that the real computational target is not “chat about trials,” but “convert unstructured trial text and patient data into auditable match evidence.”

**Molecular tumor boards and therapy recommendation.** MEREDITH, retrieval-augmented therapy suggestion for tumor boards, and the 2026 context-augmented MOAlmanac-based Cancer Cell paper form a natural sequence: from early expert-guided RAG over heterogeneous evidence, to PubMed-grounded MTB support with explicit citation auditing, to more mature precision-oncology recommendation systems built around curated clinicogenomic knowledge. These case studies are excellent for discussing what counts as “ground truth” when clinicians themselves consider multiple plausible therapies.

**Oncology text extraction and summarization.** CORAL and the UCSF pathology dataset show how oncology notes and reports can be converted into structured facts for research and operations. The PD-L1 biomarker extraction study adds a concrete real-world data curation example, while newer pathology summarization work suggests that LLMs can reduce clinician burden in compressing complex diagnostic records. This is the most natural subtopic for teaching human-in-the-loop validation and error taxonomies, because extraction errors are easy to inspect clinically.

**Guideline and patient-information question answering.** Cancer-information chatbots are an important, albeit less mature, case study because they expose multilinguality, referencing quality, and false-presupposition failures. Recent evaluations show that public-facing models now often provide references, but the references vary in quality and non-English performance remains less reliable; radiation-oncology evaluations similarly show that even strong answers can still be potentially harmful in some settings if used without supervision.

**Workflow automation and metadata curation.** Cancer-specific evidence here is still thinner than for trial matching or tumor-board support, so broader biomedical exemplars are appropriate. BioAgents and metadata harmonization work show how agentic or LLM-assisted systems can standardize metadata, assemble analysis plans, and reduce the labor of database curation. For an oncology course, these should be framed as “research infrastructure” applications with immediate relevance to cohort building, registry curation, and translational data reuse.

**Suggested three-lecture outline.**
- **Lecture one: Background and problem definition.** Start from the oncology information bottleneck: papers, trial criteria, pathology reports, clinic notes, and genomic evidence evolve faster than any static model can memorize. Introduce the shift from Week 10’s pretrained models to Week 11’s interactive systems, and map the main data substrates: literature, guidelines, trial registries, EHR text, pathology reports, and clinicogenomic knowledge bases. Use Carl, Hao, Yang, and the resource bundle around MOAlmanac/OncoKB/CIViC/PrimeKG/SPOKE.
- **Lecture two: Method development and verification.** Teach baseline RAG, hybrid retrieval, reranking, iterative retrieval, graph-backed retrieval, tool use, and planner–executor–reviewer agent patterns. Then teach verification: benchmark design, claim-level source checking, clinician rubrics, error taxonomies, and safety-aware omission/hallucination accounting. Use MedRAG/MIRAGE, i-MedRAG, RAG², GeneGPT, ESCARGOT, SourceCheckup, TREC BioGen, and CREOLA.
- **Lecture three: Applications and state of the field.** Walk through oncology case studies in increasing system complexity: CORAL and pathology extraction, TrialGPT and biomarker-aware trial matching, MEREDITH and MTB support, then the MOAlmanac-based context-augmented precision-oncology recommender. End by comparing general LLMs, RAG, and agentic systems, emphasizing that gains in accuracy do not eliminate the need for expert oversight.

## Open problems, future directions, and boundaries

The field’s most important open problem is **faithful, statement-level grounding over heterogeneous oncology evidence**. Current systems still struggle when the relevant support lives across multiple papers, trial records, approval labels, tables, or figure captions, and citation support remains unreliable even in strong web-connected models. Near-future work is likely to push toward multimodal and temporally versioned RAG, where retrieval units include tables, page images, and structured registry fields rather than text chunks alone.

A second open problem is **evaluation on real oncology work products rather than exam-style QA alone**. Systematic reviews still find methodology heterogeneity and limited standardization, while even strong oncology systems can show low “clinical use readiness” despite respectable accuracy or relevance scores. The field needs shared benchmarks based on real tumor board packets, longitudinal patient histories, protocol drafting, and trial-screening artifacts, with expert adjudication and explicit provenance scoring.

A third open problem is **privacy-preserving system design for PHI-heavy oncology corpora**. The next generation of useful systems will almost certainly combine local or institutionally hosted models, governed retrieval over protected records, and more formal privacy threat modeling across indexing, retrieval, and generation stages. This is particularly important for pathology, molecular tumor board, and registry-curation use cases, where practical accuracy and privacy are tightly coupled.

A fourth open problem is **scientific ideation and biomarker prioritization**. The literature increasingly shows LLMs helping with candidate gene prioritization, translational hypothesis generation, and metadata or literature-based curation, but cancer-specific validation remains early and the benchmarks are still weak. For teaching purposes, this area belongs in the module as an emerging frontier—students should understand the methods and evaluation pitfalls, but also see clearly that “interesting hypothesis” is not yet the same as “validated discovery.”

**What belongs in this module.** This week should own retrieval-augmented oncology systems, guideline and literature QA, document-grounded annotation, provenance and citation tracking, biomedical knowledge graphs as retrieval substrates, agentic bioinformatics workflow support, metadata curation, and human-in-the-loop validation.

**What should be left to other weeks.** Leave model pretraining, tokenizer choices, scaling laws, instruction tuning, and broad medical foundation-model benchmarking to Week 10. Leave digital twin, mechanistic simulation, and perturbation-response system modeling to Week 12. Leave prospective clinical deployment, regulation, reimbursement, enterprise governance, and formal implementation frameworks to Week 13. In other words: Week 11 should teach how to build and validate oncology-facing generative systems; it should not be the week that primarily teaches how to pretrain them or how to deploy them as regulated clinical products.

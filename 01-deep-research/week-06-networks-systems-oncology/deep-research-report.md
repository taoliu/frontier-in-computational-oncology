# Gene Regulation, Networks, and Systems Oncology

## Module overview

This module should teach students how to move from processed molecular measurements to mechanistic, system-level hypotheses about cancer regulation. The central idea is that tumors are not explained well by single genes alone. They are better described as regulatory circuits and signaling states that link genomic alterations, lineage context, microenvironmental inputs, and therapeutic pressure to transcriptional programs, cell states, and phenotypic plasticity. Methodologically, the module should distinguish undirected association from direct regulation, directionality from genuine causal claims, and static snapshots from dynamic or perturbation-supported models. The core data inputs are processed bulk and single-cell transcriptomes, chromatin accessibility or paired multiome data, phosphoproteomics, and perturbation readouts such as CRISPR or drug-response transcriptomics. The computational focus is method development and verification: network inference, pathway and regulon activity estimation, Bayesian and causal graphical models, dynamical systems, and perturbation-aware modeling, all tied to oncology applications such as oncogenic signaling, transcriptional addiction, tumor suppressor circuitry, immune evasion, and resistance.

## Must-read papers and resources

- **Badia-I-Mompel et al., 2023, *Nature Reviews Genetics*.** The best entry-point review for a graduate module. It organizes GRN inference by data modality and model family, with strong discussion of transcriptome-only versus multiome approaches, priors, and evaluation. It is especially useful for framing what single-cell and multiome data can, and cannot, identify. DOI: **10.1038/s41576-023-00618-5**.

- **Kelly et al., 2022, *Molecular Genetics & Genomic Medicine*, paired with Sachs et al., 2005, *Science*.** Kelly et al. is a concise review of causal discovery for molecular networks, covering assumptions behind Bayesian networks, graphical models, and intervention-based reasoning. Sachs et al. is the landmark signaling paper that made causal structure learning in cell biology concrete with multiparameter single-cell measurements. Together they are ideal for teaching what “causal” really means in this field. DOI: **10.1002/mgg3.2055** and **10.1126/science.1105809**.

- **Margolin et al., 2006, *BMC Bioinformatics*.** The landmark ARACNe paper. It teaches the mutual-information view of network inference and the use of the data processing inequality to prune indirect edges. It remains foundational for cancer master-regulator analysis and for understanding why coexpression is not enough. DOI: **10.1186/1471-2105-7-S1-S7**.

- **Huynh-Thu et al., 2010, *PLoS ONE*.** The GENIE3 paper is still one of the clearest formulations of GRN inference as supervised learning, where each target gene is predicted from candidate regulators using tree ensembles. It is an essential baseline for method comparison and for teaching feature-importance-based network inference. DOI: **10.1371/journal.pone.0012776**.

- **Aibar et al., 2017, *Nature Methods*, with the 2020 pySCENIC protocol.** SCENIC is the canonical regulon-first single-cell method: candidate TF-target links, motif-based pruning, then per-cell regulon activity via AUCell. It is indispensable for teaching why motif or chromatin support matters if one wants to move from correlation toward direct transcriptional regulation. For implementation, the scalable pySCENIC workflow is the practical companion. DOI: **10.1038/nmeth.4463**.

- **Alvarez et al., 2016, *Nature Genetics*.** This paper operationalized VIPER-style thinking at cancer scale by inferring protein activity from regulon targets rather than relying on regulator expression alone. It is central for teaching latent regulator activity, master regulators, and why raw mRNA abundance is often a weak proxy for signaling or TF activity. DOI: **10.1038/ng.3593**.

- **Schubert et al., 2018, *Nature Communications*.** The PROGENy paper is a must-read for pathway activity modeling because it uses perturbation-responsive downstream genes, not merely pathway membership. It is the clearest demonstration that pathway activity should often be treated as a latent variable inferred from footprints. DOI: **10.1038/s41467-017-02391-6**.

- **Liu et al., 2019, *npj Systems Biology and Applications*.** CARNIVAL is an excellent teaching paper for causal reasoning on signed, directed prior networks. It starts from expression footprints and contextualizes large signaling graphs into condition-specific causal subnetworks. DOI: **10.1038/s41540-019-0118-z**.

- **Dugourd et al., 2021, *Molecular Systems Biology*.** COSMOS goes one step beyond transcriptome-centered reasoning by integrating phosphoproteomics, transcriptomics, and metabolomics with prior knowledge to generate mechanistic hypotheses. This is a strong capstone paper for systems oncology because it shows how signaling, regulation, and metabolism can be connected causally. DOI: **10.15252/msb.20209730**.

- **Badia-i-Mompel et al., 2022, *Bioinformatics Advances*, paired with Müller-Dott et al., 2023, *Nucleic Acids Research*.** decoupleR provides a unified software framework for activity inference across many scoring methods, which is ideal for teaching reproducible comparison. CollecTRI expands signed TF-target priors and was benchmarked to improve perturbed-TF recovery, making it a strong modern prior resource for regulon-based inference. DOI: **10.1093/bioadv/vbac016** and **10.1093/nar/gkad841**.

- **Pratapa et al., 2020, *Nature Methods*, paired with Chevalley et al., 2025, *Communications Biology*.** This is the best benchmark pair to teach verification. Pratapa et al. benchmarked GRN inference from single-cell transcriptomic data. Chevalley et al. introduced CausalBench, a real-world perturbational benchmark for network inference from single-cell CRISPR data. Together they show why observational and perturbational benchmarks can yield different conclusions. DOI: **10.1038/s41592-019-0690-6** and **10.1038/s42003-025-07764-y**.

- **Paull et al., 2021, *Cell*, paired with TCGA Oncogenic Signaling Pathways, 2018, *Cell*.** Paull et al. is the best systems-oncology anchor for master-regulator analysis at pan-cancer scale, identifying 407 master regulators arranged into 24 conserved blocks controlling transcriptional identity. The TCGA signaling paper grounds these ideas in a canonical cohort-wide map of altered oncogenic pathways across more than 9,000 tumors. These papers keep the module tied to cancer biology rather than abstract network theory. DOI: **10.1016/j.cell.2020.11.045** and **10.1016/j.cell.2018.03.035**.

## Key computational problems and assumptions

The first problem is to define the object of inference. A “gene regulatory network” may mean TF-to-target transcriptional edges, generic gene-gene dependencies, pathway relations among signaling proteins, or a hybrid prior graph that mixes several layers. Students should learn to ask whether the node and edge semantics match the assay. Transcriptomics alone is strongest for transcriptional footprints. Paired RNA and chromatin accessibility improve TF-target plausibility. Phosphoproteomics is more directly aligned with upstream signaling state. Signed prior resources such as OmniPath and CollecTRI help, but they also import bias from curation and from the biological contexts represented in the literature.

The second problem is the difference between **association** and **direct regulation**. Correlation, mutual information, and tree-based feature importance can identify dependence, including nonlinear dependence, but they do not by themselves prove a direct edge. ARACNe partly addresses this by pruning indirect relations with the data processing inequality. GENIE3 improves predictive power by decomposing network inference into many target-wise regression problems, but its edges are still learned from observational dependence. This distinction is a good place to teach why good prediction can coexist with weak causal meaning.

The third problem is the jump from **dependence** to **directionality**. Orientation can come from TF inventories, motif support, chromatin accessibility, signed prior graphs, temporal information, or interventions. SCENIC uses motif pruning to move from candidate coexpression to more plausible TF-target regulation. LINGER and CellOracle push further by exploiting paired multiome or GRN-aware in silico perturbation. But directionality still depends on assumptions about motif specificity, peak-to-gene linkage, completeness of priors, and quality of the inferred cellular progression.

The fourth problem is the difference between **directionality** and **causality**. Bayesian networks, causal discovery algorithms, and difference-causal methods can orient relations under assumptions such as acyclicity, faithfulness, and adequate handling of hidden confounding. In molecular systems, those assumptions are fragile because biology is full of feedback loops, latent regulators, and mixed cell states. This is why intervention data remain so valuable. Sachs et al. is the landmark example. DCI is useful for teaching how one can infer changes between causal graphs across conditions, but not escape the need for assumptions.

The fifth problem is whether the system is **static or dynamic**. Most cancer transcriptomic datasets are snapshots. Methods like SINGE, CellOracle, and dynamical landscape models try to recover transition structure from pseudotime, trajectories, or multiome-informed flows, but their conclusions are only as good as the ordering or state manifold. Students should learn that a dynamic-looking graph inferred from cross-sectional data is still not equivalent to measured time-series causality.

The sixth problem is **validation**. Curated TF-target databases, ChIP-seq, motif occurrence, and pathway databases do not measure the same thing. Perturbation data recover intervention responses, not necessarily direct edges. Synthetic benchmarks give complete truth, but can make unrealistic assumptions. Real-world benchmarks are harsher. In CausalBench, current methods still struggle on large perturbational single-cell data, and exploiting interventional labels does not automatically outperform observational baselines. For pathway and TF activity inference, benchmark outcomes also shift with the prior resource and scoring method, which is why a modern class should teach benchmarking as part of method design, not as an afterthought.

## Main algorithms, models, and tools to teach

A useful teaching sequence starts with **expression-driven GRN inference**. ARACNe represents the mutual-information family and introduces pruning of indirect edges. GENIE3 represents tree-ensemble prediction and remains one of the strongest classical baselines. Inferelator is a good scalable regression-based framework for larger and single-cell datasets, and it exposes implementation decisions about regularization, regulators lists, and distributed computation. The pedagogical point is that these methods infer sparse predictive graphs, not automatically causal ones.

The next family is **regulon-centric and multiome-aware inference**. SCENIC remains the canonical workflow because it joins candidate TF-target links to motif support and then scores regulon activity per cell with AUCell. CellOracle adds GRN-aware in silico perturbation. LINGER uses atlas-scale external data plus paired single-cell multiome input to improve inference when within-study sample size is small. These methods are ideal for discussing implementation choices such as motif databases, candidate TF restriction, peak-to-gene linkage, pseudobulk versus single-cell fitting, and stability across random seeds or subsamples.

A separate cluster is **pathway and latent-activity inference**, which should not be conflated with edge inference. GSEA is the classical competitive gene-set enrichment framework. GSVA and related single-sample methods convert pathway sets into per-sample activity-like scores. UCell is a practical single-cell scoring method based on the Mann-Whitney U statistic. VIPER and PROGENy represent a more mechanistic idea: infer the activity of a TF or pathway from downstream footprints, not from the regulator’s own expression. decoupleR is the most convenient modern teaching platform because it exposes many of these estimators in one interface and makes method comparison reproducible.

For **Bayesian networks and causal graphical models**, the module should teach the DAG language explicitly: conditional independence, structure learning, interventional orientation, and the difference between graph recovery and hypothesis generation. Sachs et al. is the classic signaling example. Angelopoulos et al. shows that Bayesian networks can also be used in cancer genomics to model relations among driver events. DCI adds a clean example of differential causal network inference between conditions, which is especially relevant for tumor versus normal, pre- versus post-treatment, or sensitive versus resistant states.

For **causal reasoning over signed directed prior networks**, the most teachable tools are CARNIVAL, COSMOS, NicheNet, and OmniPath-backed workflows. CARNIVAL contextualizes large signaling networks from activity footprints. COSMOS stacks phosphoproteomics, transcriptomics, and metabolomics. NicheNet is especially helpful for immune-oncology because it goes beyond ligand-receptor matching and traces probable signaling and transcriptional consequences in receiver cells. OmniPath supplies the signed and directed prior knowledge layer that makes these models practical.

For **dynamic and perturbation-aware models**, the right teaching examples are nonlinear differential-equation approaches, Granger-style methods, and intervention simulators. Perturbation biology is the classic cancer example of fitting executable nonlinear signaling models from systematic drug perturbations. SINGE shows how irregular pseudotime can be handled with Granger-causality ensembles. CellOracle and melanoma transition-landscape work illustrate how state transitions can be simulated or summarized even when direct time-course measurements are incomplete. This family is where students can learn that dynamic claims require stronger assumptions, but also deliver much stronger biological hypotheses.

## Representative datasets and benchmarks

- **TCGA Pan-Cancer Atlas and the GDC** should be a core teaching dataset for cohort-scale systems oncology. They support pathway-state analysis, master-regulator analysis, and tumor-suppressor circuit analysis across many cancer types. The TCGA signaling and TP53 papers are especially useful because they show how system-level pathway interpretation can be layered on top of large multi-omic cohorts.

- **CCLE and DepMap** are the best routine resources for connecting inferred network states to functional cancer dependencies. Official DepMap releases provide harmonized mutation, copy number, whole-exome or genome sequencing, RNA-seq, fusion data, and CRISPR gene-effect scores; Chronos is the current gene-effect model. These datasets are ideal for asking whether inferred master regulators or pathways predict genetic dependency or drug sensitivity.

- **LINCS L1000 and the LINCS Data Portal** are central if the module wants a perturbation-response perspective. They provide large collections of transcriptional signatures under chemical and genetic perturbation and are especially valuable for pathway-footprint construction, regulator benchmarking, and reverse-signature reasoning.

- **Genome-scale and combinatorial Perturb-seq datasets** are now essential for causal network verification. Replogle et al. profiled more than 2.5 million cells in genome-scale single-cell CRISPRi screens, while Norman et al. used combinatorial perturbations to map rich genetic interaction manifolds. These are strong training examples for interventional evaluation and for showing why transcriptome-only edges often fail under intervention.

- **Frangieh et al. Perturb-CITE-seq** is the cleanest immuno-oncology benchmark in this space. The Broad portal exposes a processed dataset with 218,331 cells and 23,712 genes from patient-derived melanoma co-cultures, making it ideal for teaching multimodal perturbation analysis, tumor-immune circuitry, and immune-evasion mechanism discovery.

- **Prior-knowledge and validation resources** should be taught as a stack, not individually. CollecTRI and DoRothEA provide signed TF-target priors for activity inference. TRRUST v2 remains a standard curated TF-target database. KnockTF 2.0 offers perturbation-based expression profiles for TF knockdown and knockout. OmniPath integrates diverse signaling and regulatory resources for causal reasoning. scGRN provides a large reference platform of cell-type-specific single-cell GRNs, with the current release cataloging 237,051 GRNs and 62,999,692 TF-target pairs across 160 tissues or cell lines and disease contexts.

- **Benchmarks should be split by task**. For observational single-cell GRN inference, the Pratapa benchmark is still the standard teaching paper. For perturbational causal evaluation, CausalBench is the modern standard. For pathway scoring robustness on cancer expression data, the 2024 robustness study is valuable because it shows that downstream biological conclusions can change materially with method choice.

## Cancer case studies

- **Pan-cancer oncogenic signaling and transcriptional identity.** The TCGA signaling analysis mapped alterations across major oncogenic pathway classes in more than 9,000 tumors, while Paull et al. identified 407 master regulators organized into 24 conserved blocks that canalize cancer transcriptional identity across 20 TCGA cohorts. This pair is ideal for showing how genomics, pathways, and master regulators can be linked at systems scale.

- **Transcriptional addiction and master TF circuitry.** Reddy et al. used the CaCTS algorithm to prioritize candidate master transcription factors from pan-cancer expression data when ChIP-seq is unavailable, directly linking expression patterns to core regulatory circuitry. This is a good oncology example of why lineage context and network position matter more than differential expression alone.

- **Tumor suppressor pathway analysis through TP53.** The TCGA TP53 study and the cross-tumor p53 response paper are useful together because they show both the breadth of TP53 pathway alteration across cancers and the existence of a core p53 transcriptional response across oncogenic contexts. They illustrate a key module lesson: mutation status alone is not equivalent to pathway activity.

- **Melanoma plasticity and resistance as a network problem.** Wouters et al. showed recurrent melanoma cell states and phenotype switching, with regulon-level analysis identifying distinct intermediate programs. Pillai et al. then modeled resistance as trajectories on regulatory landscapes. Pozniak et al. extended this logic by uncovering a TCF4-dependent regulatory network that contributes to resistance to immunotherapy and targeted therapy. This trio is one of the strongest lecture arcs in contemporary systems oncology.

- **Immune evasion circuits from perturbation-rich data.** Frangieh et al. used multimodal Perturb-CITE-seq in patient-derived melanoma models to define immune-evasion mechanisms and identified CD58 loss as a mechanism orthogonal to canonical IFN-γ and MHC-based resistance. This is an excellent case study for the distinction between association and perturbation-supported causality.

- **Patient-specific regulatory heterogeneity in lung cancer.** scGeneRAI inferred GRNs from static single-cell RNA-seq and was applied to more than 15,000 cells from 10 NSCLC patients, recovering both known and apparently tumor- or subclone-specific network structures. This is a practical example of how single-cell networks can capture patient-specific wiring, while also raising questions about validation and generalization.

## Suggested three-lecture outline

**Lecture one: from measurements to latent regulatory activity.** Start with the biological need in cancer, where driver alterations converge on pathway and transcriptional states rather than acting as isolated genes. Introduce processed bulk RNA-seq, scRNA-seq, multiome, and perturbation-response data as inputs. Then teach the distinction between gene-set enrichment and latent activity inference using GSEA, GSVA, UCell, VIPER, PROGENy, and decoupleR. Close with ARACNe and GENIE3 as the transition from sample-level activity scoring to edge-level network inference.

**Lecture two: from association to causal and mechanistic networks.** Teach Bayesian networks, causal discovery, and structure learning through the lenses of assumptions, identifiability, and intervention. Use Sachs as the landmark causal signaling study, Kelly et al. for conceptual framing, Angelopoulos et al. for cancer-genomics-scale Bayesian networks, and DCI for condition-specific differential causality. Then move to causal reasoning over signed directed priors using CARNIVAL, COSMOS, OmniPath, and NicheNet. End this lecture with a validation discussion centered on why edge orientation is not the same as causation.

**Lecture three: dynamics, perturbations, and oncology applications.** Begin with SINGE, perturbation biology, CellOracle, and melanoma transition-landscape models to show how one can move from static graphs toward executable or transition-aware systems. Then build the cancer applications around three stories: melanoma plasticity and resistance, immune-evasion circuits from Perturb-CITE-seq, and pan-cancer master-regulator architecture from TCGA-scale data. Close with benchmarking and verification, using Pratapa et al. and CausalBench to show how real perturbational evaluation can overturn intuition from observational data.

## Open problems, future directions, and module boundaries

A central open problem is **context specificity**. Cancer regulatory networks are strongly shaped by lineage, mutation background, epigenetic state, and microenvironment. Methods such as CellOracle and LINGER are promising because they move beyond transcriptome-only inference, and scGeneRAI shows that patient- and subclone-specific networks can be recovered, but these advances also make transferability and external validation harder.

A second open problem is **evaluation under realistic ground truth**. Curated edges test literature agreement. Motifs and chromatin test regulatory plausibility. TF perturbations, CRISPR screens, and Perturb-seq test interventional consequences. None is a complete gold standard. The most important methodological shift for the next few years is likely to be away from edge-overlap alone and toward activity recovery, intervention prediction, and counterfactual validation on perturbation-rich data.

A third problem is **hidden confounding, feedback, and cyclic biology**. Many causal tools inherit DAG assumptions that are mathematically convenient but biologically restrictive. Cancer signaling is full of feedback loops, rewiring, and state-dependent interactions. This is one reason prior-knowledge causal reasoning methods and executable dynamical models remain attractive even when they are harder to fit.

A fourth problem is **single-cell sparsity and uncertainty**. Sparse counts, zero inflation, cell-state mixtures, and noisy pseudotime all degrade network inference. Large benchmarks continue to show that scalability and generalization remain difficult, and robustness studies of pathway activity inference on cancer data show that biologically meaningful conclusions can vary with scoring choices. This is a strong argument for teaching stability selection, ensemble methods, pseudobulk sensitivity analyses, and uncertainty reporting as part of the core curriculum.

A fifth direction is **integrative multi-omics and perturbation-aware systems oncology**. COSMOS, OmniPath, CellOracle, and LINGER all point toward a future in which transcriptomics, chromatin, phosphoproteomics, metabolites, and perturbations are analyzed jointly, with explicit priors and interpretable mechanistic outputs. For an advanced graduate course, this is probably the right “near-future” frontier to emphasize, more than generic black-box deep learning.

Within the course, **this module should include** system-level inference across genes, pathways, TF regulons, signaling graphs, and dynamic or perturbation-aware regulatory circuits. It should assume that students already have processed matrices or basic state labels and are ready to ask mechanistic questions. The teaching center is the model: how it represents biology, how it orients edges, what assumptions it requires, and how it is validated.

**This module should leave to other weeks** the chemistry and mechanics of assay generation, alignment and quantification pipelines, single-cell quality control, normalization, batch correction, clustering, and generic cell-type annotation, as well as CRISPR library design, screen execution, and dose-response modeling. Those topics are important, but they are prerequisites or neighboring modules rather than the intellectual center of gene regulation and systems oncology. The SCENIC protocol explicitly sits on top of standard single-cell best-practice preprocessing, and recent perturbation-modeling reviews similarly treat the experimental perturbation platform as the input rather than the main computational question.

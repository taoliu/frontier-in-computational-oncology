# Frontier in Computational Oncology Week 8 Clinical Informatics and Outcome Prediction

## Module overview

This module studies how routine oncology data become computational evidence for patient-level risk, prognosis, treatment-effect, and outcome models. The central story is that cancer EHRs, registries, linked claims, and semi-structured clinical notes are not curated “omics-style” research matrices; they are care-generated, code-mediated, irregularly sampled, and often outcome-incomplete records whose missingness can be informative, whose endpoints must be operationalized, and whose treatment choices are confounded by indication. A serious graduate treatment therefore has to connect data representation and phenotype extraction to survival analysis, competing risks, dynamic prediction, causal inference, calibration, fairness, and transportability, while also showing how modern oncology studies validate real-world endpoints such as real-world overall survival and progression-free survival against trials and chart abstraction. The module belongs squarely in computational oncology because much of the difficulty is methodological: defining time zero, handling censoring and competing events, modeling time-varying covariates, coping with coding systems and site differences, and verifying whether a model trained in one care setting remains calibrated and clinically useful in another.

## Must-read papers and resources

1. **Collins et al., 2025, *BMJ Oncology* — “Clinical prediction models using machine learning in oncology: challenges and recommendations.”** DOI: 10.1136/bmjonc-2025-000914. This is the best recent oncology-specific overview of how to build prediction models that are methodologically sound, equitable, and clinically useful; it explicitly covers missing data, censored outcomes, competing risks, fairness, calibration, external validation, and implementation barriers.

2. **Castellanos et al., 2024, *JCO Clinical Cancer Informatics* — “Raising the Bar for Real-World Data in Oncology: Approaches to Quality Across Multiple Dimensions.”** DOI: 10.1200/CCI.23.00046. A foundational paper for explaining why oncology RWD quality is multi-dimensional and “fitness for use” depends on provenance, abstraction, consistency, completeness, and endpoint-specific validation.

3. **Kehl et al., 2024, *Nature Communications* — “Shareable artificial intelligence to extract cancer outcomes from electronic health records for precision oncology research.”** DOI: 10.1038/s41467-024-54071-x. Essential for the semi-structured data portion of the week: it shows how privacy-preserving teacher–student distillation can make note-based outcome extraction models transferable across centers.

4. **Jee et al., 2024, *Nature* — “Automated real-world data integration improves cancer outcome prediction.”** DOI: 10.1038/s41586-024-08167-5. A flagship clinicogenomic informatics paper showing that NLP-derived clinical features from reports materially improve overall-survival prediction beyond stage or genomics alone, while also enabling biological discovery about metastatic tropism.

5. **Lasiter et al., 2021, *Clinical Pharmacology & Therapeutics* — “Real-world Overall Survival Using Oncology Electronic Health Record Data: Friends of Cancer Research Pilot.”** DOI: 10.1002/cpt.2443. A key endpoint-validation paper for teaching why even “simple” outcomes such as overall survival require careful data-source-specific definitions and sensitivity analyses in observational oncology data.

6. **Ton et al., 2022, *Clinical Cancer Research* — “Replication of Overall Survival, Progression-Free Survival, and Overall Response in Chemotherapy Arms of Non–Small Cell Lung Cancer Trials Using Real-World Data.”** DOI: 10.1158/1078-0432.CCR-22-0471. Important because it moves beyond rwOS to rwPFS and response endpoints, showing what real-world endpoint replication can and cannot recover from oncology EHR sources.

7. **van Amsterdam et al., 2025, *Clinical Oncology* — “Causal Inference in Oncology: Why, What, How and When.”** DOI: 10.1016/j.clon.2024.07.002. A concise, oncology-focused introduction to estimands, DAG thinking, confounding, treatment-effect heterogeneity, and when causal questions should not be answered by purely prognostic models.

8. **Hernán and Robins, 2016, *Journal of Clinical Epidemiology* — “Using Big Data to Emulate a Target Trial When a Randomized Trial Is Not Available.”** DOI: 10.1016/j.jclinepi.2016.03.001. This older landmark is still indispensable for the observational-treatment-effect part of the week because it formalizes time zero, eligibility, treatment assignment, follow-up, and causal contrasts in a way that directly prevents immortal-time and selection bias.

9. **Tsegaye et al., 2025, *Journal of Clinical Epidemiology* — “Larger sample sizes are needed when developing a clinical prediction model using machine learning in oncology.”** DOI: 10.1016/j.jclinepi.2025.111675. A valuable corrective to the common assumption that flexible ML automatically solves small-sample oncology prediction problems; it is ideal for discussing events-per-parameter, optimistic performance estimates, and underpowered subgroup analyses.

10. **TRIPOD+AI, 2024, *BMJ* — updated reporting guidance for regression- and AI-based clinical prediction models.** DOI: 10.1136/bmj-2023-078378. This is the practical reporting checklist to pair with methods papers, especially for student critique of oncology CPM manuscripts.

11. **PROBAST+AI, 2025, *BMJ* — updated quality, risk-of-bias, and applicability assessment tool for prediction models.** DOI: 10.1136/bmj-2024-082505. This is the strongest framework for structured appraisal of oncology prediction studies and for teaching why many published models are not implementation-ready.

12. **Hueting et al., 2022, *Journal of Clinical Epidemiology* — “The majority of 922 prediction models supporting breast cancer decision-making are at high risk of bias.”** DOI: 10.1016/j.jclinepi.2022.10.016. This is the sobering oncology-specific field audit: it demonstrates at scale that the literature is crowded with models but shallow in validity, transparency, and transportability.

## Key computational problems and assumptions

**Clinical data are operational records, not clean study matrices.** Diagnoses, procedures, and treatments are represented through evolving coding systems such as ICD-10-CM, SNOMED CT, and RxNorm; cancer-specific harmonization often requires oncology-aware common data models such as the OMOP Oncology extension. That means representation error, site-specific coding practice, and mapping quality are part of the modeling problem rather than mere preprocessing details.

**Missingness is often informative and sometimes plausibly missing-not-at-random.** In oncology EHRs, labs, staging fields, performance status, response assessments, and supportive-care variables are often available precisely because something clinically relevant happened, so naïve complete-case analysis or off-the-shelf imputation can distort both prediction and causal estimation. Students should explicitly distinguish MCAR, MAR, and MNAR assumptions and understand that “not recorded” can mean “not measured,” “measured elsewhere,” “clinically normal,” or “workflow omission.”

**Time alignment is central.** Nearly every important oncology question depends on defining eligibility, baseline, exposure assignment, follow-up start, and censoring correctly. If treatment starts after cohort entry or covariates are updated after outcome onset, immortal-time bias and label leakage appear immediately; if treatment is re-evaluated over time, then time-varying confounding and dynamic treatment regimes enter the problem.

**Endpoints are constructed, not simply “read off” the chart.** Overall survival can be fairly robust if death ascertainment is good, but progression, response, toxicity, hospitalization, symptom worsening, and quality-of-life deterioration are only partially standardized in routine care. Real-world progression depends on surveillance intensity, physician language, imaging frequency, and abstraction rules; competing risks matter whenever non-cancer death or treatment discontinuation can preclude the event of interest.

**Causal inference requires stronger assumptions than prognosis.** For observational treatment-effect questions, exchangeability, positivity, and consistency do not come “for free” from flexible machine learning. Strong prognostic discrimination is not evidence that a model identifies treatment benefit; causal estimands need design discipline, explicit comparators, and sensitivity to unmeasured confounding.

**Calibration, fairness, and transportability are first-class outcomes.** In oncology care, a model that discriminates well but is poorly calibrated can still mislead treatment decisions or advance-care-planning referrals. Performance must therefore be evaluated across time, sites, demographic groups, and care settings, with recalibration and updating treated as expected parts of the workflow rather than afterthoughts.

**The field’s default assumption should be external skepticism.** Large oncology reviews and validation studies show that many published models are underpowered, weakly reported, or fail to transport. This is pedagogically useful: students should learn to view benchmark and validation design as an equal partner to model design.

## Main algorithms and models to teach

**Baseline prognostic modeling.** Start with penalized logistic and Cox models, then move to flexible parametric survival models and cause-specific hazards models. These remain the clearest way to teach estimands, coefficients, shrinkage, and calibration, and they are often the best baseline rather than a straw man.

**Competing-risk and multi-endpoint survival modeling.** Teach both cause-specific hazard models and Fine–Gray subdistribution models, emphasizing that the choice depends on whether the scientific target is etiologic association or cumulative incidence for decision support. In oncology, this is especially relevant for cancer-specific death versus other-cause death, relapse versus treatment-related mortality, and progression versus non-progression discontinuation.

**Dynamic prediction with time-varying covariates.** Students should see three paradigms: time-dependent Cox models, landmarking, and joint longitudinal-survival modeling. A useful teaching sequence is static risk first, then landmarking as an operational bridge to real-world EHRs, then joint models as the principled but more structured alternative.

**Nonlinear and machine-learning survival models.** Random survival forests, boosting-style survival learners, DeepSurv, DeepHit, and Dynamic-DeepHit are worth teaching not as “better models by default,” but as examples of how nonlinear interactions, competing risks, and longitudinal structure can be modeled. Their strengths should always be discussed alongside sample-size demands, calibration behavior, and interpretability tradeoffs.

**Observational causal inference.** Core methods should include DAG-based confounder selection, propensity scores, inverse-probability weighting, overlap weighting, g-computation, marginal structural models, doubly robust estimation, and target trial emulation. For advanced students, heterogeneous treatment-effect methods and causal ML are appropriate extensions, but only after the design-stage assumptions are clear.

**Clinical NLP and phenotype extraction.** The right goal for this week is not generic note summarization; it is extracting model-ready clinical variables and endpoints from radiology reports, oncologist notes, discharge summaries, and pathology text when structured fields are incomplete. Students should compare rule-based extraction, supervised transformers, weak supervision, abstraction-assisted labeling, privacy-preserving distillation, and chart-abstraction-based ground truth.

**Verification and performance assessment.** Every modeling lecture should end with the same question: how would you know this works elsewhere? Internal validation, temporal and geographic external validation, subgroup calibration, decision-curve analysis, and risk-of-bias assessment should be taught as mandatory parts of the algorithmic pipeline, not separate “translation” topics.

## Representative datasets and benchmarks

**Population and registry data.** SEER is the canonical U.S. population-based cancer registry resource, covering about 45.9% of the U.S. population and capturing demographics, tumor site and morphology, stage at diagnosis, first course of treatment, and vital status. NCDB is a large hospital-registry resource from more than 1,500 Commission on Cancer–accredited facilities, useful for treatment-pattern and outcomes studies but less population-representative than SEER.

**Linked claims and patient-reported outcomes.** SEER-Medicare is the workhorse for elderly U.S. oncology observational studies because it links population-based cancer registry data with Medicare enrollment and claims. SEER also supports linked resources such as SEER-MHOS for health-related quality of life and function, and SEER-CAHPS/SEER-Medicaid for patient experience and Medicaid populations.

**Longitudinal oncology EHR resources.** CancerLinQ aggregates longitudinal oncology practice data at scale and now advertises more than 9 million patients across multiple EHR systems. Commercial EHR-derived datasets such as Flatiron’s research databases and clinico-genomic databases have been especially influential because they support endpoint validation, trial emulation, and structured plus abstracted oncology variables, although access is generally proprietary.

**Public clinico-genomic datasets.** AACR Project GENIE is a publicly accessible registry of real-world clinico-genomic data assembled across 20 international cancer centers, and its Biopharma Collaborative curation is directly used in modern oncology NLP and clinicogenomic studies. MSK-CHORD extends this idea with a 24,950-patient integrated dataset that combines reports, treatments, demographics, registry data, and genomics for several major cancers.

**Harmonization rather than a single dataset.** OMOP Oncology is better thought of as infrastructure for reproducible multi-institutional analysis than as a dataset in itself. It is especially useful pedagogically because it forces students to think about episodes, episode-event linking, and how metastatic disease phases or treatment courses are represented in standardized analytic form.

**Benchmarking paradigms worth teaching.** In this subfield, the strongest “benchmarks” are often methodological rather than leaderboard-style: replication of trial endpoints in RWD, comparison of ML-extracted variables to manual abstraction, and broad external validation of existing models in registries. Good exemplars are the Friends/Flatiron rwOS and rwPFS studies, ML-extracted-versus-abstracted replication analyses in lung cancer, and registry-based validation of 87 breast cancer models in 271,040 Dutch patients.

## Cancer case studies

**Advanced NSCLC as the endpoint-validation laboratory.** Much of the modern real-world endpoint literature has been built in metastatic NSCLC, where investigators have compared rwOS, rwPFS, and rw response with trial endpoints and shown that replication is feasible but sensitive to cohort construction, surveillance practices, and endpoint definitions. This is the cleanest disease area for teaching why endpoint ascertainment is itself a computational method.

**MSK-CHORD as a clinicogenomic discovery platform.** MSK-CHORD demonstrates that adding NLP-derived variables such as metastatic sites and prior treatment can materially improve survival prediction, and that outcome-oriented real-world annotation can lead to biologically interesting findings such as associations between specific alterations and site-specific metastasis. It is one of the best examples of method development enabling both better prediction and better biology.

**Short-term mortality and supportive-care triage.** Parikh and colleagues showed that combining routinely collected EHR variables with patient-reported outcomes improves short-term mortality prediction compared with either data source alone, and later work extended explainable EHR-based mortality risk models for palliative-care prompting. These studies are excellent for discussing label choice, actionability, calibration near decision thresholds, and whether better prediction meaningfully changes care.

**Symptoms, adverse events, and supportive outcomes from routine data.** Recent oncology EHR studies have predicted common cancer symptoms from structured records, extracted symptom mentions from clinical notes, and built models for chemotherapy-related adverse reactions or immunotherapy-related adverse events from routine documentation. These use cases broaden the module beyond survival to patient-centered and safety endpoints, which are often more clinically actionable but harder to define consistently.

**Breast cancer as a transportability cautionary tale.** Breast cancer has a huge literature of decision-support nomograms, yet systematic review found most models to be at high risk of bias, and large-scale external validation showed wide heterogeneity in performance. More focused validations of PREDICT in young node-negative cohorts showed underestimation of mortality and limited clinical utility, making breast cancer a useful case for discussing why publication volume does not imply deployment readiness.

**Trial generalizability and RWD-based emulation.** Trial Pathfinder and TrialTranslator show how oncology EHR data can be used to study inclusion criteria and treatment-effect generalizability in real-world populations. This is a strong state-of-the-field example because it connects prediction, causal design, and transportability in a single computational framework.

## Suggested three-lecture outline

**Lecture one — From oncology routine care data to analyzable cohorts and endpoints.** Begin with the data substrate: registries, EHRs, claims, and notes; explain coding systems, common data models, and why oncology clinical data differ from curated molecular datasets. Then move to cohort construction, phenotype definition, time zero, endpoint operationalization, missingness mechanisms, censoring, and competing risks, using SEER/SEER-Medicare and the Friends endpoint pilots as the running examples.

**Lecture two — Method development and verification for patient-level prediction.** Cover survival modeling from Cox to random survival forests and deep survival models; then introduce dynamic prediction with time-varying covariates through landmarking and joint models. The verification half of the lecture should emphasize internal versus external validation, temporal drift, calibration, subgroup fairness, net benefit, risk-of-bias tools, and sample-size requirements, with breast-cancer validation studies as the cautionary anchor.

**Lecture three — Causal inference, treatment-response modeling, and the current oncology landscape.** Frame the distinction between prognostic prediction and treatment-effect estimation; teach target trial emulation, weighting, and causal ML at a conceptually rigorous level. Close with applications in NSCLC endpoint replication, clinicogenomic outcome prediction with MSK-CHORD, symptom and adverse-event modeling, and trial generalizability, while stressing what still blocks trustworthy patient-level counterfactual decision support.

## Open problems, future directions, and boundaries

The biggest unresolved methodological issue is still *ground truth*. Real-world oncology endpoints remain sensitive to note availability, imaging cadence, death capture, and abstraction rules, so better benchmark sets with manual adjudication, multi-site replication, and surveillance-aware analyses are needed. Closely related problems are robust recalibration under dataset shift, principled fairness auditing for small intersectional subgroups, stronger handling of MNAR missingness, and better evaluation of models that estimate patient-level treatment benefit rather than only prognosis. The next wave is likely to combine target-trial discipline with richer multimodal clinical data, privacy-preserving note models, and federated/common-data-model infrastructures, but each of those advances will matter only if validation becomes more rigorous than the current literature norm.

What belongs in this week: structured and semi-structured patient data from EHRs, registries, claims, and clinical notes; patient-level risk prediction; survival and competing-risk methods; treatment-response and adverse-event modeling from routine care data; causal inference for observational oncology; and validation concepts such as calibration, transportability, and fairness. What should be left to other weeks: image-based pathology or radiology pixel models and vision encoders; general LLM agents, copilots, or autonomous assistants as a primary topic; and the full regulatory, workflow-integration, human-factors, reimbursement, and longitudinal monitoring agenda of deployed clinical systems. Note also that NLP belongs here only when it serves phenotype extraction or endpoint construction from notes and reports, not as a standalone general-purpose language-model module.

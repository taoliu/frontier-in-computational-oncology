# Digital Twins in Computational Oncology

## Module overview

This module should teach students that a **digital twin** in oncology is not just any predictive model. In the stricter sense used by the National Academies and many recent medical reviews, a digital twin is a patient-linked virtual construct that is **dynamically updated with real data, has predictive capability, and is used to inform decisions**. That makes it different from a static simulator, a one-time prognostic model, a synthetic patient, or a generic AI classifier. In oncology, the most useful teaching story is the progression from **virtual cell** ideas, to **virtual tumors** driven by mechanistic growth and therapy models, to **virtual patients** and eventually **digital twins** that assimilate longitudinal imaging, molecular, PK/PD, and clinical data to forecast future trajectories under candidate interventions. The core scientific question is not only “can we predict what will happen?” but also “what mechanism is being simulated, what uncertainties remain, and when can a simulated alternative treatment be interpreted as a credible counterfactual rather than only a plausible what-if scenario?” Recent work in glioma radiotherapy, triple-negative breast cancer, immuno-oncology QSP, and adaptive therapy makes this a timely graduate topic, but the field is still mostly in a proof-of-principle stage, with limited prospective validation and many inflated claims around the digital twin label.

## Must-read papers and resources

1. **National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine. _Foundational Research Gaps and Future Directions for Digital Twins_ (2024).** This is the cleanest starting point for definitions. It is useful for class because it makes the bidirectional physical-virtual link, predictive capability, and decision value explicit, and it helps students distinguish twins from ordinary simulation. Official report/resource.

2. **Hernandez-Boussard et al. _Digital twins for predictive oncology will be a paradigm shift for precision cancer care_. Nature Medicine (2021), doi: 10.1038/s41591-021-01558-5.** Short, influential, and still useful as a manifesto paper for oncology digital twins. Best assigned before lecture one because it motivates the field without getting lost in implementation details.

3. **Stahlberg et al. _Exploring approaches for predictive cancer patient digital twins: Opportunities for collaboration and innovation_. Frontiers in Digital Health (2022), doi: 10.3389/fdgth.2022.1007784.** A broad community paper from the NCI/DOE cancer patient digital twin effort. Very helpful for course design because it lays out pilot-project archetypes and shows how heterogeneous the term “cancer patient digital twin” already was by 2022.

4. **Mollica et al. _Digital twins: a new paradigm in oncology in the era of big data_. ESMO Real World Data and Digital Oncology (2024), doi: 10.1016/j.esmorw.2024.100056.** A concise oncology-focused review that is especially good for lecture three. It summarizes current applications, emphasizes continuous updating, and discusses ethical and practical barriers.

5. **Bunne et al. _How to build the virtual cell with artificial intelligence_. Cell (2024), paired with Karr et al. _A whole-cell computational model predicts phenotype from genotype_. Cell (2012), doi: 10.1016/j.cell.2012.05.044.** These are not oncology twins per se, but they are the right upstream readings for the “virtual cell” part of the module. Bunne et al. frame modern AI-enabled virtual cell ambitions, while Karr et al. remain the landmark whole-cell modeling example showing what mechanistic completeness might mean in principle.

6. **Lorenzo et al. _Patient-Specific, Mechanistic Models of Tumor Growth Incorporating Artificial Intelligence and Big Data_. Annual Review of Biomedical Engineering (2024), doi: 10.1146/annurev-bioeng-081623-025834.** This is likely the best single review for the module’s method-development lecture. It connects mechanistic models, patient-specific calibration, AI integration, optimization, and key barriers to clinical use.

7. **Wang et al. _From virtual patients to digital twins in immuno-oncology: lessons learned from mechanistic quantitative systems pharmacology modeling_. npj Digital Medicine (2024).** Essential for distinguishing a **virtual patient cohort** from a stricter **digital twin**. It is also one of the clearest papers on why immuno-oncology twins are often treatment- and study-specific rather than universal.

8. **Chaudhuri et al. _Predictive digital twin for optimizing patient-specific radiotherapy regimens under uncertainty in high-grade gliomas_. Frontiers in Artificial Intelligence (2023), doi: 10.3389/frai.2023.1222612.** A model paper for teaching calibration, uncertainty, and optimization. It shows how mechanistic MRI-informed tumor models, Bayesian calibration, and risk-aware treatment optimization fit together in one digital-twin pipeline.

9. **Wu et al. _MRI-based digital twins to improve treatment response of breast cancer by optimizing neoadjuvant chemotherapy regimens_. npj Digital Medicine (2025), doi: 10.1038/s41746-025-01579-1.** One of the strongest recent patient-specific oncology twin case studies. It is especially useful because it goes beyond response prediction to schedule optimization and includes retrospective validation against historical regimen comparisons.

10. **Cess et al. _Calibrating agent-based models to tumor images using representation learning_. PLOS Computational Biology (2023), doi: 10.1371/journal.pcbi.1011070.** This is a strong methods paper for lecture two. It addresses a central technical bottleneck: how to fit spatial agent-based simulations to microscopy or tissue images when the output is too rich for simple summary-statistic matching.

11. **Simeoni et al. _Predictive pharmacokinetic-pharmacodynamic modeling of tumor growth kinetics in xenograft models after administration of anticancer agents_. Cancer Research (2004), doi: 10.1158/0008-5472.CAN-03-2524.** This is the landmark older paper that still belongs in the module because modern oncology twins still rely heavily on TGI and PK/PD ideas descended from this framework. It is the right historical anchor for therapy simulation.

12. **Zhang et al. _Integrating evolutionary dynamics into treatment of metastatic castrate-resistant prostate cancer_. Nature Communications (2017), doi: 10.1038/s41467-017-01968-5, together with Zhang et al. _Evolution-based mathematical models significantly prolong response to abiraterone in metastatic castrate-resistant prostate cancer_. eLife (2022), doi: 10.7554/eLife.76284.** This pair is the clearest example of mathematical oncology moving from eco-evolutionary theory to a real treatment protocol and patient data. It is ideal for discussing what “counterfactual optimization” can and cannot mean in the clinic.

## Key computational problems and assumptions

The first problem is **state construction**. A useful oncology twin needs an internal state that is rich enough to simulate future biology but simple enough to calibrate from sparse data. Depending on the use case, that state may be tumor volume, subclone fractions, voxel-level cellularity, immune-cell densities, intracellular signaling states, or whole-body PK compartments. The choice is never neutral: it decides what future can be simulated and what cannot. Virtual-cell work makes this explicit at the molecular scale; patient-specific tumor modeling makes it explicit at tissue and patient scales.

The second problem is **observability**. Oncology data are rarely dense in time. Imaging may be available only at diagnosis and one early follow-up. Blood biomarkers can be noisy and delayed. Biopsies are sparse and confounded by sampling bias. As a result, many latent biological variables in mechanistic models are only weakly observed, which makes parameter identifiability fragile. This is why population priors, hierarchical calibration, approximate Bayesian methods, and virtual-population approaches are common.

A third problem is **prediction versus simulation versus counterfactual inference**. A predictive model can forecast an observed endpoint without representing the intervention mechanism. A simulator can generate trajectories under user-specified intervention inputs, but that alone does not make the outputs causal. A counterfactual claim requires stronger assumptions, such as correct intervention semantics in a mechanistic model or credible causal identification in observational data. Recent precision-oncology causal-ML perspectives are useful here: unlike ordinary predictive ML, causal ML is explicitly about what would have happened under treatment A versus B. That distinction should be made explicit in this module.

A fourth problem is **mechanistic explanation**. Twin models are often sold as interpretable because they contain biologically named variables, but explanation only becomes credible when those variables are structurally identifiable enough to be constrained by data and when the model can be falsified against new observations. Good fit is not enough. This issue is central in both digital twin reviews and formal validation discussions.

A fifth problem is **uncertainty**. There are at least four layers: measurement noise, parameter uncertainty, structural model error, and intervention uncertainty. For teaching, students should see that uncertainty quantification is not a decorative add-on but part of the scientific claim. In the glioma digital twin work, uncertainty is propagated into treatment optimization itself rather than only into error bars after the fact.

A sixth problem is **verification and validation**. Verification asks whether the model and code solve the equations correctly. Validation asks whether forecasts are accurate and clinically useful for the intended decision. In cancer twins, real counterfactual ground truth is usually unavailable, so fit-for-purpose validation often combines synthetic data, retrospective holdout forecasting, comparison with historical trials, matched experimental systems, and in rarer cases prospective external testing.

The last key set of assumptions concerns **exchangeability and transportability**. Virtual patient and virtual trial work often assumes that calibrated parameter distributions from one study can be reused for another therapy or cohort, or that preclinical mechanisms transfer to humans with limited retuning. These assumptions can be useful, but they should be named and stress-tested rather than hidden.

## Main algorithms models and tools to teach

- **Low-dimensional tumor growth and treatment ODEs.** Start with exponential, logistic, and Gompertz growth laws, then move to tumor growth inhibition and PK/PD systems with transit or delay compartments. The Simeoni framework is still the landmark teaching example, and recent pharmacometric oncology work continues to build on it for dose selection and response modeling.

- **Spatial PDE and mechanics-coupled models.** Reaction-diffusion, Fisher-KPP type invasion models, and mechanics-coupled voxel or continuum models are central for imaging-based virtual tumors, especially in glioma and breast MRI applications. These models are the core of recent UT Austin and related digital-twin work.

- **Agent-based and hybrid multiscale models.** These are best when local cell-cell interaction, spatial heterogeneity, tissue architecture, or immune ecology matters. For software, teach the ecosystem rather than a single package: PhysiCell for 3-D multicellular physics-based simulation, PhysiBoSS 2.0 for coupling intracellular Boolean signaling to cell agents, PhysiPKPD for pharmacology, Chaste for well-tested modular simulation, and CompuCell3D for virtual tissue models.

- **QSP and virtual-patient generation.** For immuno-oncology and virtual clinical trials, students should learn how whole-system mechanistic models are parameterized, how virtual patients are sampled or selected, and how calibration is performed against both tumor burden and survival-type endpoints. The Craig guide on virtual patients, the Wang review, and recent NSCLC QSP papers form a solid reading set.

- **Hybrid AI-mechanistic methods.** This includes Bayesian combination of mechanistic modeling and machine learning, mechanistic learning, and imaging models that combine deep neural networks with biology-based PDE or ODE simulators. These methods are worth teaching because they address a central tradeoff: purely mechanistic models often underfit, while purely statistical models struggle to support intervention simulation.

- **Calibration, sensitivity, and UQ algorithms.** Students should see MAP and full Bayesian calibration, MCMC, approximate Bayesian computation, ensemble methods, likelihood-free inference, surrogate modeling, and representation-learning-based discrepancy measures for ABM calibration. For digital twins, calibration is not one step but a sequential data-assimilation loop.

- **Optimization and control.** Teach multi-objective optimization, optimal control, adaptive therapy rules, and selective use of reinforcement learning. The point is not to present RL as magic, but to show how intervention search depends on the quality of the underlying simulator and how optimization under uncertainty differs from black-box policy learning.

## Representative datasets benchmarks and case studies

For **datasets and benchmarks**, this module should emphasize resources with longitudinal structure, multimodal linkage, or treatment context. The Cancer Imaging Archive is the main public imaging hub, and the NCI Quantitative Imaging Network is directly aimed at developing and validating quantitative imaging tools for predicting tumor response. Useful collections include **I-SPY 2** DCE-MRI for neoadjuvant breast therapy, **UPenn-GBM** for multiparametric glioblastoma MRI plus clinical and molecular data, **LUMIERE** for longitudinal GBM MRI with expert RANO assessment, **Yale-Brain-Mets-Longitudinal** for a large brain-metastasis MRI time series resource, and **RADCURE** for head-and-neck radiation-planning CT with treatment context. For evolutionary and longitudinal molecular data, **TRACERx** is the flagship resource. For preclinical validation and therapy testing, **PDMR** and **PDXNet** remain important repositories of patient-derived models and associated molecular data.

For **radiation-response case studies**, the clearest example is the high-grade glioma digital twin of Chaudhuri et al., which used a mechanistic MRI-informed tumor model, Bayesian personalization, and risk-aware optimization. In its in silico cohort of 100 patients, the framework produced patient-specific radiotherapy options that traded off tumor control against toxicity, including a reported median time-to-progression gain of about six days at the same total dose, or a median dose reduction of 16.7% for comparable control. This is a strong teaching example because it makes uncertainty, optimization, and treatment recommendations inseparable.

For **chemotherapy and neoadjuvant therapy**, the recent triple-negative breast cancer work is particularly strong. Wu et al. used 105 ARTEMIS trial patients, calibrated biology-based twins to patient MRI, achieved pCR prediction with AUC 0.82, and then searched 128 schedule options to identify patient-specific regimens, with retrospective evidence that optimized schedules improved expected pCR rates and reproduced known historical regimen advantages. A related hybrid study by Stowers et al. combined deep learning and a biology-based model to predict TNBC NAC response from pretreatment MRI alone, which is a good example of hybrid mechanistic learning in practice.

For **immunotherapy**, Butner et al. built a mechanistic model of tumor response to immune checkpoint inhibition and reported reliable description of immune response and long-term tumor burden across 124 patients, four cancer types, and two immunotherapy agents, with validation cohorts from four additional clinical trials totaling 177 patients. In parallel, Johns Hopkins QSP work on NSCLC generated virtual patients using immunogenomic data from iAtlas plus durvalumab PK information, predicted an overall response rate of 18.6% with bootstrap interval 13.3% to 24.2%, and suggested CD8/Treg ratio as a biomarker beyond PD-L1 and TMB. These papers work well together because one focuses on patient-specific response quantification and the other on cohort-level virtual-patient generation.

For **adaptive therapy and tumor evolution**, metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer remains the canonical case. The 2017 Nature Communications paper formulated the eco-evolutionary treatment logic, and the 2022 eLife follow-up connected that logic to an adaptive abiraterone pilot, showing that model-guided intermittent therapy could prolong response while reducing drug exposure. This is a good place to teach the difference between mechanistically justified protocol design and generic response prediction.

For **virtual clinical trials and multiscale tumor-immune simulation**, two examples are especially useful. First, the 2024 NSCLC QSP virtual-trial study simulated a conditionally activated PD-L1 targeting antibody and shows how virtual-patient cohorts can be used before individualized twin deployment. Second, Rocha et al. built a multiscale micrometastasis immune-surveillance model and explored more than 100,000 virtual patient trajectories, illustrating how multiscale virtual populations can reveal regime structure before true patient-specific calibration is possible.

## Suggested lecture outline

**Lecture one: concepts, biology, and problem definition.** Start by defining digital twin, virtual patient, virtual tumor, and virtual cell, and make students say clearly what counts as a twin and what does not. Then cover the biological and clinical need: forecasting tumor response, optimizing therapy schedules, anticipating resistance, and simulating future disease trajectories under intervention. Close by contrasting four claims: prediction, forward simulation, causal counterfactual, and mechanistic explanation. Good anchor readings are the National Academies definition, Hernandez-Boussard, Stahlberg, Mollica, and the Bunne virtual-cell perspective.

**Lecture two: method development, calibration, and verification.** Organize the lecture around model families: growth and PK/PD ODEs, spatial PDEs, agent-based models, QSP, and hybrid AI-mechanistic systems. Then move to patient-specific calibration, identifiability, Bayesian updating, approximate Bayesian computation, representation-learning distances for spatial models, and uncertainty propagation. Verification and validation should be explicit topics, not an afterthought. Good readings are Lorenzo, Cess, Craig, Simeoni, PhysiCell plus ecosystem papers, and the formal validation chapter by Lorenzo and colleagues.

**Lecture three: oncology applications and state of the field.** Use three to five case studies: glioma radiotherapy twins, breast MRI-guided chemotherapy twins, immunotherapy/QSP virtual patients, adaptive therapy in prostate cancer, and multiscale micrometastasis models. End with a sober discussion of what has actually been validated, what remains retrospective or in silico, and why many current “digital twins” are really treatment-specific personalized simulators rather than continuously updating clinical twins. Good readings are Chaudhuri, Wu, Wang, Zhang, and a recent limitations-focused precision-medicine review.

## Open problems and future directions

The most immediate open problem is **credibility**. Many papers use the term “digital twin” for anything from a fitted mechanistic model to a statistical policy model, but recent scoping reviews explicitly exclude generic classifiers, synthetic patients, and models not linked to a specific patient. For teaching, this is an advantage: students can learn to ask whether a system is truly personalized, dynamically updated, and decision-facing, or whether it is better described as a simulator, virtual patient, or risk model.

A second open problem is **prospective validation**. The field has many retrospective or in silico demonstrations, but comparatively little prospective evidence showing that twin-guided decisions improve outcomes. Recent oncology and precision-medicine reviews repeatedly identify this gap, and the medical digital twin literature increasingly emphasizes fit-for-purpose validation, regulatory framing, and data architecture rather than only algorithmic novelty.

A third open problem is **multiscale coupling**. Virtual-cell efforts are advancing quickly, but clinically useful cancer twins need to bridge intracellular regulation, cell-state transitions, tissue geometry, immune ecology, drug PK/PD, and patient-level outcomes. Reviews in mechanistic learning, virtual-cell AI, and mathematical oncology all point to this same challenge: scale integration is scientifically attractive but computationally and statistically hard.

A fourth open problem is **causal use under intervention**. Digital twins are often marketed as if every simulated alternative therapy were a valid counterfactual. In reality, counterfactual use requires either strong mechanistic confidence that the intervention is represented faithfully, or formal causal assumptions that justify treatment-effect interpretation from the data. This is where links to causal ML, target-trial thinking, and intervention-specific validation will likely become more important over the next few years.

A fifth open problem is **computational speed without loss of trustworthiness**. Patient-specific calibration of PDEs, ABMs, and QSP models can be too slow for clinical use. Near-future directions include differentiable simulators, reduced-order models, surrogates, GPU-parallel frameworks such as TumorTwin, and hybrid AI-mechanistic systems that accelerate calibration while retaining intervention semantics and uncertainty estimates.

## Module boundaries

This week **belongs to simulation of future biological or clinical states under interventions**. It should include mechanistic tumor growth models, agent-based and multiscale models, PK/PD and QSP, patient-specific calibration, uncertainty quantification, adaptive therapy, and virtual clinical trials when they arise from explicit treatment-response simulators. It should also include the conceptual distinction between virtual cell, virtual tumor, virtual patient, and digital twin, plus the difference between forecasting, simulation, and causal counterfactual reasoning.

This week should **not** become a duplicate of adjacent modules. It should leave **perturbation-screen modeling and large-scale genotype-to-drug-response mapping** to the perturbation/functional-genomics week; it should leave **EHR-only prognostic scores, observational treatment-response models, and generic survival prediction** to the clinical prediction week unless those models are embedded in a forward simulator; and it should leave **LLM agents, conversational copilots, and generative workflow systems** to the AI-agents week. A practical rule is simple: if the method does not maintain a patient-linked latent dynamical state and cannot simulate treatment-conditioned future trajectories with explicit uncertainty, it is probably adjacent material rather than core material for this module. Recent digital-twin definitions and scoping reviews support exactly this narrower reading.

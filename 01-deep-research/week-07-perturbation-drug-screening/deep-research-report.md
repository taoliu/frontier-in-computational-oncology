# Frontier in Computational Oncology Week Seven Drug Screening Perturbation Modeling and Virtual Cell Models

## Module overview

This module should teach students how oncology perturbation experiments become computational objects and, eventually, predictive models: pooled CRISPR knockout/interference/activation screens and legacy RNAi assays turn perturbations into guide-count data; pharmacologic screens turn perturbations into dose–response surfaces; chemogenetic and synthetic-lethality screens ask how genetic context changes drug response; Perturb-seq and related multimodal assays replace scalar phenotypes with transcriptomic, proteomic or chromatin-state readouts; and emerging “virtual cell” efforts try to infer unmeasured perturbation responses in silico. The biological aim is to discover dependencies, rational combinations, resistance mechanisms and context-specific vulnerabilities in cancer, but the computational difficulty is that every stage is noisy and design-dependent: multiplicity of infection, library coverage, controls, guide efficiency, off-target activity, copy-number and proximity artifacts, baseline fitness differences, batch effects, limited dose grids, non-unique synergy null models, noisy single-cell perturbation labels, and poor cross-context generalization can all dominate the signal if they are not modeled explicitly. This week therefore belongs squarely to perturbation-driven response modeling, not to general network inference without interventional data and not to patient-level digital twins.

## Must-read papers and resources

- **Bock et al., _High-content CRISPR screening_ (Nature Reviews Methods Primers, 2022; DOI: 10.1038/s43586-021-00093-4).** The best single methods-oriented primer for designing CRISPR screens, understanding pooled versus high-content phenotypes, and connecting experimental decisions to downstream computation.

- **Bodapati et al., _A benchmark of algorithms for the analysis of pooled CRISPR screens_ (Genome Biology, 2020; DOI: 10.1186/s13059-020-01972-x).** Essential for Lecture Two because it compares algorithmic families under simulation and real-data settings and makes benchmarking logic itself part of the curriculum.

- **Vinceti et al., _A benchmark of computational methods for correcting biases of established and unknown origin in CRISPR-Cas9 screening data_ (Genome Biology, 2024; DOI: 10.1186/s13059-024-03336-1).** A must-read on copy-number and proximity biases, which are now impossible to ignore in cancer dependency analysis.

- **Arafeh et al., _The present and future of the Cancer Dependency Map_ (Nature Reviews Cancer, 2025; DOI: 10.1038/s41568-024-00763-x).** The clearest recent perspective on what dependency maps can support biologically, how they are used in oncology, and what the next generation of perturbation resources needs to add.

- **Pacini et al., _A comprehensive clinically informed map of dependencies in cancer cells and framework for target prioritization_ (Cancer Cell, 2024).** The most directly relevant modern pan-cancer dependency resource paper, linking CRISPR screening with multi-omics and target prioritization across 930 cancer cell lines.

- **Corsello et al., _Discovering the anti-cancer potential of non-oncology drugs by systematic viability profiling_ (2020).** The key PRISM resource paper for pooled-cell-line pharmacologic screening, drug repurposing, and computational prediction of selective response from molecular features.

- **Subramanian et al., _A Next Generation Connectivity Map: L1000 Platform and the First 1,000,000 Profiles_ (Cell, 2017; DOI: 10.1016/j.cell.2017.10.049).** Still the landmark perturbational-transcriptomics paper; assign it to explain why response signatures, not only viability, matter for mechanism-of-action and perturbation matching.

- **Replogle et al., _Mapping information-rich genotype-phenotype landscapes with genome-scale Perturb-seq_ (Cell, 2022).** The definitive genome-scale Perturb-seq paper and a bridge from classical screen analysis to representation learning on high-dimensional perturbation phenotypes.

- **Wei et al., _Benchmarking algorithms for generalizable single-cell perturbation response prediction_ (Nature Methods, 2025; DOI: 10.1038/s41592-025-02980-0).** The benchmark to use when teaching how to evaluate perturbation-response models honestly across unseen cell states and unseen perturbations.

- **Wooten et al., _MuSyC is a consensus framework that unifies multi-drug synergy metrics for combinatorial drug discovery_ (Nature Communications, 2021; DOI: 10.1038/s41467-021-24789-z).** The paper to assign when students ask what “synergy” actually means and why a single scalar answer is usually inadequate.

- **Bunne et al., _How to build the virtual cell with artificial intelligence_ (Cell, 2024; DOI: 10.1016/j.cell.2024.11.015).** The most useful forward-looking paper for ending the module with a rigorous, not hand-wavy, discussion of virtual perturbation concepts.

- **Coelho et al., _Base editing screens define the genetic landscape of cancer drug resistance mechanisms_ (Nature Genetics, 2024; DOI: 10.1038/s41588-024-01948-8).** A strong application paper showing how perturbation design itself changes the computational problem, moving from gene knockout to variant-level resistance mapping.

## Computational problems assumptions and methods

The heart of the module is to make students see that perturbation datasets are not interchangeable. A pooled CRISPR dropout screen, a CRISPR-plus-drug modifier screen, a cell-line pharmacologic screen, a Perturb-seq experiment, and a virtual perturbation benchmark all require different observation models, different null assumptions, and different validation strategies. Strong teaching in this week should repeatedly connect the experimental design to the statistical model and then to the benchmark.

**Key computational problems and assumptions**

- **Pooled CRISPR and RNAi hit calling.** sgRNA or shRNA count changes are typically modeled as overdispersed count data with normalization, replicate structure and guide-to-gene aggregation; methods such as MAGeCK and MAGeCK-VISPR use negative-binomial style modeling plus robust rank aggregation or likelihood-based estimation, while BAGEL/BAGEL2 uses reference essential and non-essential gene sets to compute Bayes factors. The crucial assumptions are that library representation is adequate, controls define a usable null, and multiple guides provide partly redundant measurements of the same latent gene effect.

- **Guide efficiency off-target activity and structural bias.** Cancer screens are strongly distorted by guide-dependent activity, multi-targeting, copy-number amplification artifacts, and proximity effects from local genome structure. CERES explicitly corrects copy-number-associated false positives, Chronos models population dynamics rather than only endpoint depletion, and recent benchmarks show that both copy-number bias and proximity bias materially change downstream biological conclusions if left uncorrected. Historically, DEMETER2 played a similar role for RNAi by integrating multiple datasets while addressing off-target seed effects and batch variation.

- **Chemogenetic and synthetic-lethality inference.** A drug-gene screen is not just a standard screen with an extra covariate: the model must separate baseline fitness, treatment effect, and interaction effect. DrugZ is still a practical canonical method because it computes gene-level Z-scores tuned for treated-versus-control CRISPR modifier screens. For synthetic lethality, the core statistical problem is even harder because “negative” labels are uncertain, effects are context dependent, and benchmarking studies show that negative-sample construction and training-data quality can dominate apparent machine-learning performance. In practice, computational synthetic-lethality models should be taught as prioritization engines for experiments, not as stand-alone truth generators.

- **Dose–response modeling and response metrics.** Many screens still summarize drug effect by IC50 or AUC after fitting sigmoidal Hill-type curves, but those summaries can be confounded by growth rate, incomplete curves, and assay design. GR metrics correct for division-rate confounding, NDR explicitly uses both positive and negative controls, hierarchical Bayesian models can propagate uncertainty across experiments, and newer multi-output Gaussian-process models predict the full response curve at all doses rather than a single summary scalar. A central conceptual point for students is that endpoint viability, growth inhibition, and full dose–response curves are related but not equivalent prediction targets.

- **Drug synergy is model-dependent, not an observed primitive.** Bliss, Loewe, HSA and ZIP each define a different null for “no interaction,” and MuSyC shows why potency synergy and efficacy synergy should be distinguished rather than collapsed. SynergyFinder 3.0 is useful pedagogically because it operationalizes this idea in a reproducible analysis environment and adds consensus scoring and multi-sample comparison. Students should leave the module understanding that the “best” synergy metric depends on the biological question, the dose matrix, and whether the aim is mechanistic interpretation or prioritization.

- **Single-cell perturbation modeling.** Perturb-seq breaks the simple “one perturbation, one scalar phenotype” paradigm: perturbation labels may be noisy, editing may be incomplete, readouts are high-dimensional, and treated/control cells are usually unpaired. scGen assumes latent perturbation vectors that can transfer across contexts; CPA makes perturbation effects compositional across dose, time and combinations; GEARS injects graph prior information to predict unseen gene combinations; CellOT explicitly maps unpaired distributions under perturbation; and pertpy offers a modern framework that bundles analysis of knockouts, drug screens and combinatorial perturbations. But recent benchmarks also show that context generalization remains the hard part, and that sophisticated models often fail to outperform simple baselines in realistic settings.

- **Virtual perturbation and virtual cell models.** This is the right week to introduce the concept, but not to teach it as solved. The “virtual cell” agenda is now explicit in both review and challenge papers, and large perturbation models are beginning to learn joint representations across perturbations, contexts and readouts. At the same time, multiple 2025 benchmarks and the Virtual Cell Challenge organizers’ own summary make clear that first-generation foundation models still do not consistently beat naive or linear baselines across important perturbation tasks. That tension between excitement and benchmark realism is pedagogically valuable.

**Main algorithms models and tools to teach**

A strong lecture set would explicitly teach the following families rather than treating them as a shopping list of software: MAGeCK and MAGeCK-VISPR for count-based CRISPR analysis and QC; MAGeCKFlute for downstream normalization and visualization; BAGEL2 for gold-standard-based essentiality scoring; CERES and Chronos for dependency estimation under cancer-specific structural biases; DEMETER2 as the historical RNAi analogue; DrugZ for chemogenetic interaction screens; Hill/4PL fitting plus IC50/AUC/AAC versus GR and NDR response summaries; hierarchical Bayesian and multi-output probabilistic models for full dose–response inference; Bliss, Loewe, HSA, ZIP, MuSyC and SynergyFinder for combination analysis; and, on the single-cell side, scGen, CPA, GEARS, CellOT, pertpy and benchmark suites such as scPerturBench. The key teaching move is to explain what statistical assumption each method makes, what nuisance it tries to remove, and what benchmark it should be judged on.

## Datasets benchmarks and cancer case studies

**Representative datasets and benchmarks**

The canonical public data stack for this module is now clear. For pooled cancer dependency screens, DepMap and the second-generation dependency map provide CRISPR-derived dependencies integrated with CCLE-style molecular characterization, including a clinically informed map across 930 cancer cell lines; CCLE itself remains the core multi-omics characterization layer for more than a thousand cancer cell lines. For pharmacologic perturbation, GDSC screens more than 1,000 genomically characterized cancer cell lines against a wide range of anti-cancer therapeutics; CTRPv2 provides 481 compounds across 860 deeply characterized cell lines; and PRISM offers pooled viability data for 4,518 compounds across 578 cell lines in the primary screen plus deeper secondary screens with fitted dose–response parameters. These are precisely the datasets students should use when stress-testing drug response models because they also expose assay heterogeneity and cross-study nontransferability. PharmacoDB 2.0 is worth teaching as an integration layer spanning GDSC, PRISM and related studies.

For perturbational transcriptomics and high-content perturbation modeling, LINCS/CMap remains foundational, with the L1000 platform and the first million-plus profiles still central to signature matching and mechanism-of-action work. For single-cell perturbation analysis, Replogle’s genome-scale Perturb-seq dataset provides more than 2.5 million cells, while scPerturb standardizes public single-cell perturbation datasets for benchmarking. PerturbDB and related databases now curate dozens of Perturb-seq studies and millions of cells, and scPerturBench formalizes benchmark datasets and evaluation for context and perturbation generalization. These resources are especially valuable because they make it possible to teach not just model fitting, but model failure analysis.

**Cancer case studies**

One excellent oncology case study is **melanoma immune evasion mapped by Perturb-CITE-seq**. In patient-derived co-culture models, multimodal Perturb-CITE-seq identified mechanisms of resistance to T-cell killing and highlighted **CD58 loss** as an immune-evasion mechanism that is distinct from the canonical B2M/HLA class I axis. This case is ideal for showing why response modeling sometimes requires protein and transcript readouts rather than viability alone, and why single-cell resolution changes the mechanistic conclusions.

A second case study is **combination discovery from genetic perturbation under drug selection**. Lee et al. built a CRISPR–drug perturbational map to identify druggable knockouts that sensitize cells to common chemotherapeutics such as doxorubicin, topotecan and cisplatin, while Tiedt et al. integrated CRISPR screening with drug profiling to identify combination opportunities around EGFR, ALK and BRAF/MEK inhibitors. Together these studies show how the computational problem shifts from “find essential genes” to “estimate interaction effects under therapy,” which is conceptually closer to translational oncology.

A third case study is **prospective resistance mapping with base editing**. Coelho et al. used base-editing mutagenesis screens in lung, colon and Ewing sarcoma models to identify resistance variants to ten targeted agents, demonstrating that resistance prediction can move from gene-level essentiality to variant-level causal inference. This is methodologically useful because it forces students to distinguish knockout screens from precise mutational perturbation screens and to think about validation at amino-acid resolution.

A fourth case study is **synthetic lethality in pan-cancer target nomination**. Historically, Project DRIVE used deep RNAi screens to uncover dependencies and synthetic-lethal relationships, and more recent work such as SCHEMATIC used multilineage combinatorial CRISPR screening to identify 1,805 actionable synthetic-lethal interactions, many previously unreported. At the same time, machine-learning benchmarks for synthetic lethality prediction show that data curation and negative-label design strongly affect apparent performance, which makes this a perfect topic for discussing why causal perturbation data still matter even in the age of graph neural networks.

A fifth case study is **patient-derived organoid pharmacotyping in pancreatic cancer**. Recent work shows that in vitro PDO pharmacotyping can inform PDAC therapy selection and that matching patients to regimens predicted to be active in organoids was associated with improved outcomes including survival. This is an important bridge from cell-line perturbation modeling toward more clinically relevant preclinical models, but it also exposes the remaining bottlenecks: organoid establishment success, time-to-result, regimen realism, and incomplete modeling of the tumor microenvironment.

## Suggested three lecture outline

**Lecture One: Background and problem definition.** Start with the oncology need: why cancer researchers run perturbation experiments at all, and why observational omics alone are often insufficient for target discovery, resistance mapping and rational combinations. Then walk through the assay landscape in a methods-first way: pooled CRISPR knockout/interference/activation, legacy RNAi, pharmacologic viability screens, chemogenetic screens, LINCS/CMap perturbational transcriptomics, Perturb-seq and organoid pharmacotyping. The lecture should foreground design variables that later become model assumptions: MOI, library coverage, positive and negative controls, replicate structure, treatment window, dose grid, readout modality and choice of model system. Recommended anchor readings are Bock 2022, Arafeh 2025, Pacini 2024 and the PRISM/GDSC/CTRP resource papers.

**Lecture Two: Method development and verification.** This lecture should be explicitly statistical. Begin with count-based screen analysis and the major algorithmic families: MAGeCK-style negative-binomial and rank-aggregation models, reference-based Bayesian scoring with BAGEL2, bias correction with CERES and related methods, population-dynamics modeling with Chronos, and DrugZ for treated-versus-control interaction screens. Then move to pharmacologic response modeling: Hill curves, IC50/AUC versus GR and NDR, uncertainty-aware and hierarchical models for full dose–response fitting, and finally synergy models from Bliss/Loewe/ZIP to MuSyC and SynergyFinder. Close by teaching what counts as convincing verification: simulations, gold-standard essential and non-essential sets, orthogonal validation, cross-study evaluation, and bias benchmarks.

**Lecture Three: Applications and the state of the field.** Use oncology applications to synthesize the module: Perturb-CITE-seq in melanoma immune evasion, CRISPR–drug combination maps for chemotherapy and targeted therapy, variant-level resistance discovery with base-editing screens, synthetic-lethality nomination across contexts, and organoid pharmacotyping in PDAC. End by tracing the arc from measured perturbations to predicted perturbations: scGen, CPA, GEARS and CellOT as increasingly ambitious response models; pertpy and scPerturBench as practical infrastructure; and the current virtual-cell agenda as a forward-looking but benchmark-constrained direction rather than a finished technology. This lecture should explicitly discuss why recent evaluations still find simple baselines hard to beat and why better benchmarks, richer contexts and more realistic readouts are now the field’s central methodological problem.

## Open problems future directions and module boundaries

The field’s biggest open problem is not simply “better deep learning,” but **better perturbation benchmarks and more faithful observation models**. Across both bulk drug-response prediction and single-cell perturbation prediction, recent studies show that within-study evaluation is often overly optimistic, assay-specific artifacts remain strong, and sophisticated models can underperform tuned baselines or even trivial means when tested under stricter splits. Near-term progress is likely to come from richer perturbation atlases, models that jointly capture response magnitude and uncertainty over full dose or state trajectories, improved handling of guide penetrance and off-target structure, stronger domain adaptation between cell lines, organoids and patient material, and more clinically meaningful combination benchmarks. On the virtual-cell side, the trend is unmistakable — large perturbation models, challenge frameworks and commercial or nonprofit virtual-cell efforts are accelerating — but the evidence base still says that benchmark realism must outrun model hype. DepMap’s announced Phase Three direction toward resistance, surface targets and higher-dimensional readouts is a good indicator of where the practical cancer-perturbation field is going next.

For course design, **this module should include** perturbation-driven functional genomics, pharmacologic screening, dose–response fitting, drug–gene interaction analysis, synergy modeling, Perturb-seq and other high-content perturbational assays, organoid response profiling, and virtual perturbation models at the cell or cell-state level. **It should exclude** general network or pathway inference that is not anchored in perturbation data, because those ideas fit better with a dedicated systems or network week; it should also exclude patient-level digital twins, longitudinal treatment simulation and clinical decision-support frameworks, because those are distinct modeling problems with different data, validation standards and translational stakes. The clean conceptual boundary is simple: this week is about learning causal response structure from experimental perturbations, not about reconstructing all of cell biology or simulating an individual patient.

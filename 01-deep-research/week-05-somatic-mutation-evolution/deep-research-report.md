# Somatic Mutation, Variant Interpretation, and Cancer Evolution

## Module overview

This module should teach a full computational story: how bulk tumor DNA sequencing, usually from paired tumor-normal whole-genome, whole-exome, or targeted-panel data, is converted into somatic SNVs and indels, copy-number states, purity and ploidy estimates, structural variation calls when needed, driver hypotheses, mutational-process assignments, and finally clonal architectures and evolutionary histories. The central method-development lesson is that each step is statistically entangled with the others: variant evidence depends on assay noise and artifact models, observed allele fractions depend on purity and allele-specific copy number, driver discovery depends on robust background-rate modeling, and clonal inference is only as good as the copy-number correction and sampling design that feed it. Good teaching examples therefore come from workflows and benchmarks, not just from headline tools: the current GATK somatic workflow formalizes assembly-based calling plus contamination and orientation-bias filtering, the GDC explicitly publishes multiple pipelines rather than pretending a single best caller exists, SEQC2 and related truth sets expose how assay and pipeline choices change accuracy, and PCAWG and TRACERx show how mutation calling, copy number, signatures, and phylogenetics combine to reveal ongoing selection, whole-genome doubling, and metastatic trajectories. citeturn39view0turn35view0turn40search0turn40search1turn41search4turn41search3

**GATK Somatic Short Variant Discovery with the MuTect lineage.** The Broad workflow is the clearest current teaching resource for Mutect2-era calling: local reassembly in active regions, Pair-HMM read-to-haplotype likelihoods, Bayesian somatic scoring, explicit contamination estimation, orientation-bias learning, probabilistic filtering, and annotation. Pair it with the original MuTect paper to show what problems the field was trying to solve in impure, heterogeneous tumors before assembly-based Mutect2 generalized the approach. **Strelka2.** Kim et al. made Strelka2 one of the core comparisons for any lecture on somatic SNV and indel detection. It is useful pedagogically because it represents a different design choice from Mutect2, with a fast candidate-generation and scoring framework that remains strong in both germline and somatic settings. **SEQC2 truth sets and best-practice benchmarking.** Fang et al. established the HCC1395/HCC1395BL reference samples and community call sets for benchmarking, while Xiao et al. turned those materials into a systematic assessment of how purity, input, assay type, and pipeline choice affect accuracy and reproducibility. This pair is the best bridge from “algorithm” to “verification.” Fang et al., *Nature Biotechnology* 2021, doi: 10.1038/s41587-021-00993-6; Xiao et al., *Nature Biotechnology* 2021, doi: 10.1038/s41587-021-00994-5. citeturn40search0turn40search1

**dNdScv and modern positive-selection analysis.** Martincorena et al. turned dN/dS ideas into a practical framework for somatic driver discovery, and the dNdScv package remains a strong default for teaching how recurrence is converted into evidence of positive selection after correcting for sequence context and mutation-rate heterogeneity. ## Key computational problems and assumptions

At the calling stage, the main problem is to distinguish true tumor-specific alleles from sequencing error, mapping artifacts, oxidation or FFPE damage, contamination, and germline variation. Current best-practice tumor-normal workflows therefore do not stop at raw calling. They add contamination estimation, orientation-bias learning, probabilistic filtering, and often panels of normals or recurrent-artifact filters. The GDC’s continued decision to publish outputs from multiple somatic pipelines rather than a single consensus is itself a useful teaching signal that no caller dominates all contexts or variant classes. citeturn39view0turn35view0turn12search4

Observed variant allele fraction is not the same thing as clone frequency. It is confounded by tumor purity, local total and allele-specific copy number, whole-genome doubling, mutation multiplicity, and subclonality. Copy-number tools therefore assume piecewise-constant segmentation of depth ratio and B-allele frequency, then search for integer states and mixture proportions that explain those signals. The hard part is identifiability: multiple purity-ploidy solutions can explain the same data, especially in single-sample bulk sequencing and in low-purity tumors. Joint multi-sample methods improve this, but they still rely on assumptions about shared breakpoints and the consistency of clonal mixtures across samples. citeturn20search27turn20search2turn40search2turn20search0

Driver discovery has its own background-model problem. Simple recurrence is misleading because mutation rate varies by trinucleotide context, gene length, chromatin state, replication timing, transcription, and process-specific signature exposure. MutSigCV solved part of this by building covariate-aware background mutation rates, whereas dNdScv reframed the question through maximum-likelihood dN/dS estimates and generally offers stronger calibration in modern evaluations. Whole-genome settings then push the field toward methods such as OncodriveFML, OncodriveCLUSTL, and DriverPower that combine burden, clustering, and functional-impact evidence. citeturn24search0turn22search1turn24search6turn24search20turn22search3turn22search11turn21search4

Mutational-signature analysis assumes that an observed mutation catalog can be represented as a non-negative mixture of latent or reference signatures. That assumption is powerful, but fragile when mutation counts are low, when callers introduce context-biased false positives, or when reference catalogs are over-constrained or misapplied. Recent fitter comparisons show that attribution results can change meaningfully across tools, so teaching should stress exposure uncertainty and model selection, not just heat maps. Copy-number and structural-variant signatures extend the same idea from point-mutation context to segmented genome architecture, and they are increasingly important for chromosomal-instability biology. citeturn34search4turn34search6turn34search7turn20search12turn38view0

Clonal and phylogenetic inference adds another layer of non-uniqueness. Bulk methods typically cluster mutations that appear to share corrected cancer cell fractions, then search for trees consistent with ancestry constraints. Many models rely on an effective infinite-sites assumption for SNVs, but real tumors also show parallel copy-number evolution, mirrored subclonal allelic imbalance, and repeated whole-genome-doubling events. Benchmarking has shown that single-sample reconstructions tend to underestimate heterogeneity, that upstream mutation and copy-number calling strongly influence downstream trees, and that algorithm choice matters more than many tumor features. citeturn25search3turn27search3turn27search10turn41search10turn19search2turn26search26turn29search1

## Main algorithms, models, and tools to teach

For **somatic SNV and indel calling**, the course should teach families rather than a single winner. Mutect2 is the best example of assembly-based haplotype calling with Bayesian somatic odds, explicit contamination handling, and artifact-aware filtering. Strelka2 offers a contrasting high-performance probabilistic design. VarScan2 remains useful as a historically important pileup-based, count-driven approach that also spans somatic CN analysis, which helps students see how older heuristic frameworks differ from assembly-based modern callers. In research settings, ensemble or consensus strategies remain common because different callers fail in different ways, which is why MC3 and the GDC expose multi-caller outputs rather than a single “truth.” citeturn39view0turn1search12turn1search10turn12search4turn35view0turn23search10

For **driver discovery and variant interpretation**, teach three layers. First, cohort-level positive-selection methods such as MutSigCV and dNdScv. Second, extension methods such as DriverPower, OncodriveFML, and OncodriveCLUSTL that add functional impact and positional clustering, especially relevant for whole genomes. Third, mutation-level prioritization and structured interpretation, using CHASMplus for driver missense scoring; ClinGen oncogenicity standards for evidence weighting; and resources such as IntOGen, OncoKB, CIViC, and CancerVar for curated or hybrid interpretation. This stack shows students the difference between statistical driver discovery, computational pathogenicity modeling, and expert evidence curation. citeturn24search0turn22search1turn21search4turn22search3turn22search11turn25search4turn28search18turn37view0turn36view0turn16search1turn41search1

For **mutational processes**, the core mathematical idea is non-negative matrix factorization or related constrained decomposition of mutation catalogs. Students should learn COSMIC SBS, DBS, and indel signatures first, then newer CN and SV signatures. For practical work, the SigProfiler suite is the simplest default for extraction and assignment, while MuSiCal is a good paper for the current state of method development and recent benchmarking. TrackSig is an elegant way to connect signature activity to evolution rather than only to static samples. citeturn34search4turn38view0turn34search17turn34search6turn18search1turn20search12

For **copy number, purity, ploidy, and structural variation**, teach the common signal model before teaching software names. The main signals are depth ratio and B-allele frequency, followed by segmentation, integer-state fitting, and clone-mixture deconvolution. Good tools to compare are ASCAT and TITAN as landmarks, HATCHet and HATCHet2 for modern multi-sample or clone-specific inference, and CNAqc as a verification layer that checks whether SNV VAFs are quantitatively consistent with reported CNA states. When relevant, add SV callers such as Manta, SvABA, and GRIDSS2 to show how discordant pairs, split reads, and local assembly support rearrangement calls. citeturn20search27turn20search2turn40search2turn20search14turn40search7turn7search24turn7search26turn7search1

For **clonal architecture, phylogeny, and timing**, a compact teaching set is PyClone-VI for clustering corrected cancer cell fractions, PhyloWGS and Treeomics for tree inference from bulk sequencing, MEDICC2 for copy-number phylogenies that are whole-genome-doubling aware, MutationTimeR for relative ordering of point mutations and gains, and TrackSig for shifting mutational processes along pseudo-time. Benchmark papers then help students see where these methods fail, especially on single-sample data or when upstream calls are unstable. citeturn27search5turn27search3turn27search10turn41search10turn18search0turn18search1turn29search2turn29search1

## Representative datasets and benchmarks

The most useful **calling benchmark** remains the SEQC2 HCC1395/HCC1395BL matched tumor-normal reference set. It is attractive for teaching because it is realistic, heterogeneous, and accompanied by both reference call sets and papers that explicitly dissect how purity, assay choice, and bioinformatics pipelines change performance. citeturn40search0turn40search1

For **multi-caller cohort resources**, TCGA MC3 is still important because it turned roughly 10,000 TCGA samples into a harmonized mutation call set using an ensemble of seven callers with artifact filtering, producing about 3.5 million somatic variants for PanCanAtlas analyses. The GDC then made an important but different design choice, namely to distribute outputs from three somatic pipelines separately so that users can study disagreement rather than inherit hidden consensus assumptions. citeturn12search0turn12search4turn35view0

For **whole-genome pan-cancer analysis**, PCAWG is the anchor dataset. Its integrated analysis spans 2,658 tumor-normal whole genomes across 38 tumor types, and the ITH paper by Dentro et al. adds subclonal annotations, driver timing, and mutational-process dynamics that are ideal for advanced assignments. citeturn12search5turn41search4

For **evolution and multi-region design**, TRACERx is the best bulk-sampling resource in lung cancer. The 2023 TRACERx paper analyzed 1,644 tumor regions from 421 prospectively enrolled NSCLC patients, making it unusually strong for teaching why regional sampling changes what one can infer about branching, WGD, and relapse. citeturn30search2turn41search3

For **metastatic whole-genome resource work**, the Hartwig Medical Database is currently one of the strongest public-facing examples, with WGS and linked clinical data from over 8,000 patients with metastatic cancer and an openly documented production pipeline. This pairs well with the 2019 metastatic pan-cancer WGS paper and the 2023 primary-versus-metastatic comparison paper. citeturn38view1turn12search14turn30search3

For **structural-variant benchmarking**, COLO829/COLO829BL is the main short-read era truth set to know. The 2022 cell-line reference study defined a curated set of 68 somatic SVs across multiple sequencing technologies and used it to evaluate performance as a function of purity and depth, which is ideal for showing how hard SV benchmarking is compared with SNV benchmarking. citeturn33search1turn33search2

For **subclonal reconstruction benchmarking**, the DREAM SMC-Het effort and its follow-up studies remain essential. The 2020 community standardization paper formalized evaluation metrics and realistic simulation, the 2021 benchmarking paper used HeteroGenesis and concluded that upstream caller choice matters greatly, and the 2025 crowd-sourced benchmark expanded to 31 algorithms on 51 simulated tumors and again found that algorithm choice substantially alters performance. citeturn29search3turn29search4turn29search1

For **reference knowledge resources**, the current IntOGen release dated 2024-09-20 includes 87 cancer types, 271 cohorts, 33,218 samples, and 633 driver genes; COSMIC currently serves mutational signatures version 3.6; and OncoKB provides curated biological and clinical implications for over 5,000 cancer-gene alterations. These are not raw benchmark datasets, but they are the right reference layers for modern variant interpretation exercises. citeturn37view0turn38view0turn36view0

## Cancer case studies

A strong **pan-cancer case study** comes from PCAWG ITH. Dentro et al. showed that nearly all informative tumors in PCAWG had detectable subclonal expansions, with frequent branching relationships, positive selection on subclonal drivers, and dynamic changes in signature activity across subclonal expansions. This paper is ideal for showing that the endpoint of mutation calling is not a list of variants but a model of ongoing evolution. citeturn41search4

For **non-small cell lung cancer**, TRACERx is the best single disease example. Frankell et al. analyzed 1,644 regions from 421 patients and reported that subclonal WGD occurred in 19% of tumors, with multiple parallel subclonal WGDs in 10%; subclonal, but not truncal, WGD was associated with shorter disease-free survival, and copy-number heterogeneity was associated with extrathoracic relapse within one year after surgery. This makes NSCLC the clearest way to connect CNA evolution to clinical trajectory without turning the module into a treatment-response week. citeturn30search2turn30search6

For **metastatic colorectal cancer**, two studies work well together. Mendelaar et al. analyzed 429 WGS metastases and found major patient groups defined by mutational signatures from primary CRC biology, prior treatment, and metastasis-specific processes. Dang et al., using multi-region primary and metastatic sampling, showed both monoclonal and polyclonal seeding and also cases consistent with metastasis-to-metastasis spread. Together they teach the difference between cohort-level metastatic landscape analysis and patient-level seeding reconstruction. citeturn17search10turn17search18

For **multiple myeloma**, timing is the main lesson. Maura et al. used serial WGS and public exome data to reconstruct driver-event hierarchy and subgroup structure, while Rustad et al. used 89 whole genomes and 973 exomes to time mutational processes and identify eight signatures, including therapy-related melphalan exposure. Myeloma is therefore an excellent disease for teaching mutation-time estimation and the idea that clinically observed relapse is only the late segment of a much longer genomic history. citeturn31search2turn32search2

For **therapy-driven evolution**, metastatic breast cancer provides a clean mutational-process example. A 2025 study found APOBEC-associated signatures enriched in post-treatment samples and implicated APOBEC3 mutagenesis in therapy resistance, while pan-cancer primary-versus-metastatic WGS analyses provide the broader frame that treated metastatic tumors are evolutionarily distinct from mostly untreated primary tumors. This pair motivates why clonal evolution and mutational-process inference matter biologically even when the module stops short of treatment recommendation. citeturn17search0turn30search3

## Suggested three-lecture outline

**Lecture A: from assay to candidate somatic calls.** Start with the biological and clinical need to identify tumor-acquired DNA alterations, then compare tumor-normal WGS, WES, and targeted panels as measurement systems with different resolution for SNVs, indels, CNAs, SVs, and mutational signatures. The core technical content should be read evidence, active-region reassembly, matched-normal logic, recurrent artifacts, contamination, FFPE orientation bias, and why VAF alone is not biology. Required readings can be the GATK somatic workflow, the MuTect landmark, Strelka2, and the SEQC2 reference-sample papers. citeturn39view0turn2search9turn1search12turn40search0turn40search1

**Lecture B: from call sets to interpretable alteration states.** Focus on method development and verification: background mutation-rate models, MutSigCV versus dNdScv, signature decomposition, purity-ploidy-CNA inference, and the need to benchmark every layer. A good teaching arc is to move from positive selection and mutational signatures into copy number, because students then see how false positives, poor segmentation, or wrong integer states propagate into driver claims and clone inference. Readings can center on dNdScv, Alexandrov signatures, MuSiCal, HATCHet, and CNAqc, with a short discussion of IntOGen, COSMIC, ClinGen, CIViC, and OncoKB as structured reference layers rather than black boxes. citeturn22search1turn34search4turn34search6turn40search2turn40search7turn37view0turn38view0turn28search18turn16search1turn36view0

**Lecture C: from alteration states to cancer evolution.** Cover cancer cell fraction, clonal versus subclonal events, tree constraints, mutation timing, whole-genome doubling, and metastatic seeding. PyClone-VI, PhyloWGS or Treeomics, MEDICC2, TrackSig, and MutationTimeR together give a coherent toolbox for explaining what can and cannot be inferred from bulk sequencing. The lecture should end with case studies from PCAWG, TRACERx, metastatic CRC, and myeloma timing, plus a benchmark discussion showing why single-sample and poorly calibrated pipelines systematically distort evolutionary conclusions. citeturn27search5turn27search3turn27search10turn41search10turn18search1turn18search0turn41search4turn41search3turn17search10turn32search2turn29search1

## Open problems, future directions, and boundaries

The biggest open problem is still **joint uncertainty propagation**. Most practical workflows call variants, segment copy number, infer purity and ploidy, assign signatures, cluster CCFs, and then build trees as largely separate steps, even though each stage changes the assumptions of the next. Benchmarks keep showing the consequence: upstream mutation and copy-number choices strongly affect subclonal reconstruction, and recent large-scale DREAM and follow-up benchmarking still find no universally best algorithm. This is especially acute in low-purity tumors, tumor-only analyses, FFPE material, low-VAF subclones, and short-read structural variation. Recent benchmarking and validation tools such as SEQC2, COLO829 SV truth sets, and CNAqc are important progress, but they do not solve the underlying identifiability problem. citeturn29search3turn29search4turn29search1turn40search0turn33search1turn40search7

Near-future directions that are worth mentioning, but not letting dominate the week, are **long-read and single-cell DNA sequencing, better SV and ecDNA modeling, longitudinal liquid-biopsy evolution, and AI-assisted interpretation with calibrated uncertainty**. On the methods side, long-read and multi-platform work is already improving somatic SV truth sets and revealing events missed by short reads. On the interpretation side, machine-learning systems such as CHASMplus and CancerVar are useful, and emerging validation studies suggest that AI driver predictions can capture real-world biology, but they still depend heavily on curated priors, training labels, and explicit standards such as the ClinGen oncogenicity framework. The correct teaching message is that AI is becoming an evidence-combining layer, not a replacement for statistical modeling or benchmarked truth data. citeturn33search2turn25search4turn41search1turn15search5turn28search18

Within the larger course, this module should **include** DNA-level calling and interpretation of SNVs, indels, CNAs, relevant SVs, purity and ploidy, mutational signatures, clonal or subclonal decomposition, phylogenetic modeling, whole-genome doubling, evolutionary timing, resistance evolution as a genomic process, and AI-assisted prioritization of oncogenic DNA alterations. It should **not spend major time** on regulatory-function interpretation of noncoding elements beyond what is required for whole-genome driver significance testing, because detailed regulatory modeling belongs with earlier genomics weeks; it should **not turn into a systems biology week**, so network propagation and pathway-level dynamical modeling should stay minimal; it should **not become a treatment-response or clinical decision-support week**, so OncoKB, CIViC, and related knowledgebases should be used here as structured evidence resources for interpretation, not as the main framework for therapy matching or reporting pipelines. citeturn21search4turn36view0turn16search1

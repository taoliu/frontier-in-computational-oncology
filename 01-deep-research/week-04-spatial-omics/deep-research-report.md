# Spatial Omics of the Tumor Microenvironment

## Module overview

This module should tell a clear methodological story: dissociated single-cell atlases explain *who* is present in tumors, but spatial omics is needed to explain *where* cells, states, and signals are organized, and how that organization shapes invasion, immune exclusion, stromal barriers, hypoxia, and therapy response. Compared with dissociated single-cell assays, spatial molecular profiling introduces new computational problems tied to assay physics and geometry: mixed spots in sequencing-based assays, sparse molecule counts at very high resolution, cell segmentation and transcript-to-cell assignment in imaging assays, registration across serial sections, reference-based deconvolution, spatial statistics, neighborhood and niche detection, and cell-cell communication constrained by distance and tissue architecture. Across transcriptomics, proteomics, and emerging spatial epigenomics, the main teaching goal is not tool operation but model design: which assumptions are imposed by each assay, how those assumptions enter algorithms, and how one can verify that inferred spatial biology is real rather than an artifact of resolution, panel design, segmentation, or reference mismatch.

## Must-read papers and resources

- **Elhanani, Ben-Uri, Keren. *Spatial profiling technologies illuminate the tumor microenvironment*. Cancer Cell, 2023. DOI: 10.1016/j.ccell.2023.01.010.** A strong entry-point review for the oncology framing of spatial technologies, what types of biological questions they answer, and where the field was heading in cancer by 2023.

- **You et al. *Systematic comparison of sequencing-based spatial transcriptomic methods*. Nature Methods, 2024. DOI: 10.1038/s41592-024-02325-3.** Essential for teaching assay tradeoffs. It benchmarked 11 sequencing-based methods across 35 experiments from three reference tissues and showed that effective resolution depends not only on feature size but also on diffusion and capture behavior.

- **Wang et al. *Systematic benchmarking of imaging spatial transcriptomics platforms in FFPE tissues*. Nature Communications, 2025. DOI: 10.1038/s41467-025-64990-y.** A head-to-head comparison of Xenium, MERSCOPE, and CosMx on matched FFPE tissue microarrays, valuable for discussing sensitivity, specificity, concordance with orthogonal data, and platform choice in cancer studies.

- **Moncada et al. *Integrating microarray-based spatial transcriptomics and single-cell RNA-seq reveals tissue architecture in pancreatic ductal adenocarcinomas*. Nature Biotechnology, 2020. DOI: 10.1038/s41587-019-0392-8.** A landmark cancer paper for the “from dissociated cells back to tissue” narrative, and still a good teaching case for reference-guided deconvolution and spatial mapping in a stromal-rich tumor.

- **Cable et al. *Robust decomposition of cell type mixtures in spatial transcriptomics*. Nature Biotechnology, 2022. DOI: 10.1038/s41587-021-00830-w.** The canonical RCTD paper. A must-read for statistical modeling of spot mixtures, technology effects, and reference-based decomposition.

- **Kleshchevnikov et al. *cell2location maps fine-grained cell types in spatial transcriptomics*. Nature Biotechnology, 2022. DOI: 10.1038/s41587-021-01139-4.** A key Bayesian method paper for fine-grained abundance mapping that explicitly models technical variation and borrows strength across locations, widely used in tumor microenvironment studies.

- **Petukhov et al. *Cell segmentation in imaging-based spatial transcriptomics*. Nature Biotechnology, 2022. DOI: 10.1038/s41587-021-01044-w.** The Baysor paper. This is the right paper for teaching why segmentation is not a preprocessing footnote but a core inferential problem in imaging-based assays.

- **Singhal et al. *BANKSY unifies cell typing and tissue domain segmentation for scalable spatial omics data analysis*. Nature Genetics, 2024. DOI: 10.1038/s41588-024-01664-3.** Important for the current shift from “cell typing first, domains second” toward models that learn cell identity together with neighborhood context.

- **Varrone et al. *CellCharter reveals spatial cell niches associated with tissue remodeling and cell plasticity*. Nature Genetics, 2024. DOI: 10.1038/s41588-023-01588-4.** A leading computational paper for scalable niche discovery and cross-sample niche comparison, directly relevant to tumor-immune-stromal neighborhood analysis.

- **Cang et al. *Screening cell-cell communication in spatial transcriptomics via collective optimal transport*. Nature Methods, 2023. DOI: 10.1038/s41592-022-01728-4.** A strong methods paper for teaching how spatial constraints change ligand-receptor inference, especially under competition among ligands and receptors.

- **Keren et al. *A Structured Tumor-Immune Microenvironment in Triple Negative Breast Cancer Revealed by Multiplexed Ion Beam Imaging*. Cell, 2018. DOI: 10.1016/j.cell.2018.08.039.** Older than the preferred window, but still a landmark cancer application showing that spatial organization itself carries prognostic information beyond cell counts.

- **Schürch et al. *Coordinated Cellular Neighborhoods Orchestrate Antitumoral Immunity at the Colorectal Cancer Invasive Front*. Cell, 2020.** Another landmark application, foundational for the cellular-neighborhood concept in oncology and still highly teachable for neighborhood graphs and spatial ecosystems.

## Key computational problems and assumptions

Spatial omics should be taught as a family of assay-specific inverse problems rather than a single analysis pipeline. In sequencing-based transcriptomics, the central issue is that a measured location may represent multiple cells, sometimes with additional transcript diffusion or ambient contamination, so the model must infer latent composition from mixed counts. In imaging-based assays, the issue is different: coordinates can be near single-molecule and near single-cell, but gene panels are targeted, transcript detection is incomplete, and assigning molecules to cells depends on segmentation or segmentation-free approximations. Proteomic imaging brings yet another regime, where the number of analytes is smaller but protein localization and phenotype definitions can be sharper. Emerging spatial epigenomics adds sparse regulatory measurements and currently has fewer mature cancer applications and fewer established computational standards.

The most important assumptions to make explicit in class are these. First, **reference validity**: deconvolution and label-transfer methods usually assume that the single-cell reference contains the major cell types and states present in the spatial sample, and that reference and target are not too distorted by disease, treatment, or platform effects. Second, **spatial stationarity or locality**: many domain, niche, or communication methods assume that nearby cells or spots are more similar or more likely to interact than distant ones, but the relevant spatial scale is biology-dependent and often user-defined by radius or k-nearest-neighbor choice. Third, **segmentation fidelity**: in imaging assays, downstream cell typing, neighborhood analysis, and interaction inference all inherit segmentation errors. Fourth, **expression-to-function assumptions**: ligand-receptor expression does not imply signaling, and proximity does not imply communication. Fifth, **histology alignment assumptions**: registration methods assume deformable correspondences across slices, yet tumors often have partial overlap, missing tissue, and strong local distortions.

Verification is unusually hard because ground truth is often incomplete. Real tumors rarely provide true cell-type proportions for each spot, true cell boundaries for each molecule, or true physical communication edges between cells. As a result, the field relies on a mix of simulated data, pseudo-spots pooled from single-cell profiles, partial gold standards from targeted single-cell assays, orthogonal imaging validation, and biological plausibility checks. This makes benchmark design itself part of the science, not just a downstream convenience.

## Main algorithms, models, and tools to teach

For **deconvolution and reference mapping**, the core methods to teach are RCTD, cell2location, DestVI, CARD, Tangram, stereoscope, and CytoSPACE. Methodologically, these span count-based probabilistic modeling, Bayesian hierarchical inference, latent variable models for continuous cell states, graph- or regression-based approaches, and optimization-based mapping of single cells to space. A useful teaching point is that benchmark results are not fully stable across datasets: one large benchmark ranked CARD, cell2location, and Tangram highly, while Spotless found cell2location and RCTD strongest overall and showed that a simple regression baseline beat many dedicated methods. That contrast is itself pedagogically valuable because it exposes the dependence of “best method” on benchmark design and data regime.

For **cell segmentation and molecule-to-cell assignment**, teach Baysor, JSTA, ComSeg, and FICTURE together because they represent four distinct philosophies: probabilistic joint modeling of morphology and transcript composition, joint segmentation plus cell-type annotation with a reference prior, point-cloud segmentation without strong cell-shape priors, and segmentation-free factorization at ultra-high resolution. This is where students can see clearly that “single-cell resolution” is not the same thing as “error-free cell definition.” In dense tumors, especially fibrotic or irregular tissue, segmentation can be the dominant source of bias.

For **spatial domains, niches, and neighborhoods**, BayesSpace, SpaGCN, STAGATE, BANKSY, CellCharter, lisaClust, and spicyR are the most teachable set. BayesSpace is useful for spot-level and subspot modeling in lower-resolution assays; SpaGCN and STAGATE show graph neural network and graph attention approaches; BANKSY unifies cell typing with domain segmentation by augmenting intrinsic features with neighborhood features; CellCharter emphasizes scalable niche discovery and cross-sample comparison; lisaClust and spicyR introduce more classical spatial-statistical viewpoints for clustering local association profiles or testing differences in co-localization across groups. Benchmarks consistently show that no single domain method wins everywhere, which is a good reason to teach model classes rather than only specific software names.

For **spatial statistics, gradients, and spatially variable genes**, a focused set is SPARK-X, nnSVG, PROST, Moran’s I style autocorrelation, and gradient-focused analyses. Students should learn the difference between global spatial variation, domain-marker genes, and cell-type-specific spatial variation, because these are often conflated in practice. They should also see that gradient analysis is not just a prettier heatmap, but a model choice about directionality, coordinate systems, and local versus global smoothness.

For **cell-cell interaction inference and multiview contextual modeling**, COMMOT, MISTy, Squidpy, and Giotto are the core tools. COMMOT is especially useful for teaching explicit spatial constraints through optimal transport; MISTy is useful for the idea of decomposing intra-cellular, juxtacrine, and broader tissue-context effects; Squidpy and Giotto are important because they package neighborhood graphs, spatial autocorrelation, co-occurrence, image linkage, and interaction routines in practical Python and R ecosystems.

For **registration and multi-slice integration**, teach PASTE, PASTE2, STalign, GPSA, and STAligner. PASTE gives a clean optimal-transport formulation for serial slices; STalign and GPSA show more flexible nonlinear coordinate warping; recent benchmarking found that PASTE2 and SPACEL handled partial overlap better for spot-to-spot alignment, while STAligner often performed well on layer-wise alignment but was weaker for fine local geometry. This is a good place to teach the difference between “alignment,” “integration,” and “3D reconstruction,” which are often mixed together in the literature.

For **software ecosystems and reproducible implementation**, Giotto, Squidpy, and the *Orchestrating Spatial Transcriptomics Analysis with Bioconductor* resource are the most useful course anchors, because they expose students to data structures, interoperability, workflow composition, and assay-specific branches for sequencing-based versus imaging-based data.

## Representative datasets, benchmarks, and cancer case studies

### Representative datasets and benchmarks

For method development and assignments, five benchmark/resource families stand out. The **sequencing-platform benchmark** by You et al. compared 11 sequencing-based methods over 35 experiments from three reference tissues and is ideal for discussing assay tradeoffs, effective resolution, and fair metric design. The **imaging-platform FFPE benchmark** by Wang et al. compared Xenium, MERSCOPE, and CosMx on matched tissue microarrays spanning 33 tumor and normal tissue types and more than 5 million cells, which is especially relevant for cancer biobanks. The **Nature Communications deconvolution benchmark** evaluated 18 methods over 50 simulated and real datasets. **Spotless** provides a reproducible benchmark pipeline with 63 silver standards, 3 gold standards, and melanoma and liver case studies. Finally, the **Genome Biology clustering/alignment/integration benchmark** evaluated 16 clustering, 5 alignment, and 5 integration methods across 68 slices from 10 public datasets, providing a strong basis for method-comparison assignments.

For durable cancer data resources, **HTAN** is the most important umbrella resource to expose students to. As of its 2025-11-26 release, HTAN reported 14 atlases, 20 organs, 2,372 cases, 10,585 biospecimens, and 31 assay types, including Visium, Xenium, Slide-seq, IMC, MERFISH, GeoMx, CODEX, and MIBI. This scale matters because near-future spatial oncology will increasingly depend on multi-assay atlas resources rather than single-study datasets. For reproducible training workflows, the OSTA book is currently one of the best living resources, with explicit chapters for sequencing-based and imaging-based spatial omics, cross-platform analysis, registration, imputation, and integrated Xenium plus Visium workflows.

A practical course dataset set could therefore combine one **method benchmark** dataset, one **spot-based cancer** dataset, one **imaging-based cancer** dataset, and one **multiplex proteomics** dataset. Concretely, that could be a Visium or Visium HD colorectal cancer slide, the Moncada or Hwang pancreatic cancer datasets, a Xenium or CosMx FFPE tumor section from the Wang benchmark cohort, and either the TNBC MIBI data from Keren or the CRC CODEX data from Schürch.

### Cancer case studies

**Triple-negative breast cancer.** Keren et al. used MIBI-TOF to quantify 36 proteins at subcellular resolution in 41 TNBC patients and showed that the tumor-immune microenvironment was structured in cellular composition, spatial arrangement, and regulatory state. The key teaching point is that prognosis was not explained only by immune abundance; the *geography* of the tumor-immune interface mattered.

**Colorectal cancer invasive front.** Schürch et al. used CODEX to define coordinated cellular neighborhoods at the invasive front of CRC, making “cellular neighborhoods” a central concept in spatial oncology. This is a particularly good case for teaching neighborhood graphs, multicellular modules, and how invasive-front organization can encode antitumoral immunity.

**Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma under treatment pressure.** Hwang et al. profiled 43 PDAC specimens with snRNA-seq and whole-transcriptome digital spatial profiling and identified a neural-like progenitor malignant program enriched after chemotherapy and radiotherapy and associated with poor prognosis. Integrating single-cell and spatial profiles resolved three multicellular communities, classical, squamoid-basaloid, and treatment-enriched, which makes this a very strong case study for therapy-associated ecosystem remodeling rather than only cell-intrinsic resistance.

**Oral squamous cell carcinoma and invasion fronts.** Arora et al. showed that tumor core and leading-edge architectures differed in transcriptional programs, neighboring cell composition, and ligand-receptor interactions, and that a leading-edge signature was associated with worse clinical outcomes across cancers. This is one of the cleanest cancer examples for spatial gradients and frontier-versus-core analysis.

**High-definition colorectal cancer immune niches.** Oliveira et al. used Visium HD on FFPE CRC and identified distinct macrophage niches, notably SPP1+ and SELENOP+ macrophage populations with different pathway enrichments, neighboring tumor programs, and predicted ligand-receptor interactions. They also localized clonally expanded cytotoxic T cells near macrophage-rich CXCL9/CXCL10/CXCL11 niches, providing a clear example of how higher spatial resolution changes the biological story one can tell.

**Melanoma and immunotherapy response.** Bai et al. identified two immune-checkpoint-resistant spatiotypes in adolescent and young adult melanoma, a Treg-rich SIL-high state and an immune-poor SIL-low state, and derived a “young immunosuppressive melanoma” score with predictive value for ICI response. This is an excellent translational case for spatial biomarkers of therapy response.

**Hypoxia gradients and memory of past microenvironments.** Godet et al. used spatial transcriptomics in breast cancer models to show that cells which had experienced hypoxia but relocated into better oxygenated regions retained hypoxia-inducible and NF-κB-associated programs. This is a strong example for teaching that spatial gradients can reflect both current position and prior ecological history.

## Suggested three-lecture outline

### Opening lecture

Start with the biological and clinical problem: why dissociated single-cell data are insufficient for tumors whose behavior depends on organization at invasion fronts, stromal barriers, immune niches, and hypoxic gradients. Then present the assay landscape in a physically grounded way: spot-based whole-transcriptome methods such as Visium and Slide-seq, imaging-based transcriptomics such as MERFISH, Xenium, and CosMx, proteomic imaging methods such as CODEX, IMC, and MIBI, and the emerging spatial epigenomics layer through spatial-ATAC-seq and FFPE-compatible variants. Close with the core computational challenges introduced by each regime: mixtures, sparse targeted panels, segmentation, registration, neighborhood scale, and communication inference.

### Methods lecture

Organize the second lecture around model classes rather than software menus. Cover deconvolution and reference mapping first, using RCTD, cell2location, DestVI, Tangram, and CytoSPACE to compare count models, priors, latent states, and optimization objectives. Then move to imaging-centric analysis, where segmentation and transcript assignment become the core model design problem, using Baysor, JSTA, ComSeg, and FICTURE as contrasting examples. Finish with spatial-domain and niche methods, from BayesSpace and graph models to BANKSY and CellCharter, and then discuss how one verifies them through simulation, pseudo-spots, orthogonal assays, and benchmark studies such as Spotless and BenchmarkST.

### Applications lecture

Use cancer-focused case studies to show how methods change biological conclusions. A strong sequence is TNBC by MIBI for tumor-immune architecture, CRC by CODEX for coordinated neighborhoods at the invasive front, PDAC under neoadjuvant treatment for multicellular community shifts, OSCC for leading-edge programs, Visium HD CRC for macrophage and T-cell niches, and melanoma for spatial biomarkers of ICI resistance. End with the state of the field: atlas-scale resources such as HTAN, rising FFPE compatibility, cross-platform study design, and near-future movement toward spatial multi-omics and spatial foundation models, while emphasizing the unresolved validation problem.

## Open problems, future directions, and module boundaries

Three open problems deserve special emphasis. First, **ground truth remains weak**. Deconvolution, domain detection, and communication inference still rely heavily on simulated or partial standards, and benchmark papers repeatedly show rank instability across data regimes. Second, **uncertainty is under-modeled**. Many pipelines output crisp neighborhoods, communication edges, or deconvolved proportions without calibrated confidence intervals that propagate segmentation, alignment, and reference uncertainty. Third, **cross-platform and multi-omic integration is still immature**, especially when moving from fresh-frozen to FFPE material, from transcript to protein or chromatin, or from spot-based to molecule-level assays. Spatial epigenomics is particularly promising, but cancer-focused applications and benchmarks are still sparse compared with spatial transcriptomics and spatial proteomics.

Near-future directions that are worth highlighting in a graduate course are these: scalable segmentation-free or weakly supervised methods for billion-molecule data; methods that jointly model RNA, protein, and chromatin in the same or matched sections; better deformable registration and 3D reconstruction across partial overlaps; causal or perturbation-aware models that go beyond proximity-based interaction inference; and clinically robust FFPE pipelines with transparent quality control and reporting standards. The recent rise of higher-resolution platforms such as Visium HD and the growing role of single-cell-scale imaging in archived tissues make these problems immediately relevant for oncology rather than speculative.

For course boundaries, **this week should include** molecular spatial assays and the computational methods needed to recover spatial cell states and ecosystems: deconvolution, segmentation and assignment, niche and domain detection, gradients, neighborhoods, spatial statistics, registration, and cell-cell interaction inference in the tumor microenvironment. **It should not absorb Week 3**, which should focus on dissociated single-cell and single-nucleus analysis without spatial structure, such as clustering, batch correction, trajectory inference, and reference construction in isolation. **It should also not absorb Week 9**, which should focus on pathology image analysis and morphology-first models, such as H&E-only feature extraction, whole-slide prognostic deep learning, or general digital pathology pipelines. Histology belongs here only when it is used as covariate or alignment information for *molecular* spatial assays, not as a standalone morphology topic.
